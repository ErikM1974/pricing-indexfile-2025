# The Critical Role of Order Approvals in Custom Apparel Production

When you’re getting custom apparel made, whether for your business, an event, or a team, the excitement of seeing your [logo on a t-shirt or jacket](https://nwcustomapparel.net/services/logo-embroidery/) is undeniable. But before production begins, there's a crucial step that can make or break your entire order: the **order approval** process. Don't see this as a formality; it's a vital safety net that protects you and the custom apparel company, ensuring your order is produced exactly as you want it.

At Northwest Custom Apparel, we've seen it all, and we know that a careful, diligent review of your approvals is the single most important action you can take to guarantee a perfect order. This guide will walk you through the entire process, explain what you need to look for, and show you why a robust approval system is a sign of a high-quality, professional apparel company.

## Understanding the Different Types of Approvals

A professional apparel company will usually provide you with two key documents for review. These are the checkpoints designed to catch mistakes before they become expensive problems.

### 1. The Order Approval

This is the master document that outlines every detail of your purchase. It’s the final contract that everyone signs off on before any work begins. It should include:

- **The Product:** Specific item numbers, brand names (like Port & Company, Hanes, or [Carhartt](https://nwcustomapparel.net/why-is-carhartt-so-popular/)), and fabric details.
- **Sizes and Quantities:** A breakdown of how many of each size you are ordering. It’s easy to make a mistake here, so check every line.
- **Garment Colors:** The exact color code or name for each piece of apparel.
- **Pricing:** A clear, itemized breakdown of costs, including any setup fees for **screen printing** or [digitizing for **custom embroidery**](https://nwcustomapparel.net/services/custom-embroidery/).
- **Shipping Address & Timeline:** The final destination for your completed order and the estimated date of delivery.

Reviewing this document carefully can prevent you from receiving 50 large t-shirts when you needed 50 medium ones or getting the wrong shade of blue for your company's logo.

### 2. The Art Approval (or Mock-up)

This is a visual representation of your design on the actual garment. It's the most important part of the process, as it shows you exactly what the final product will look like. The art approval should include:

![A digital mock-up of a custom t-shirt on a computer screen, with annotations showing the logo's precise size, placement, and Pantone colors as part of the art approval process.](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2019/02/art-approval-mockup.webp)

The art approval is your final look at the finished product. Don’t approve anything without a clear, detailed mock-up that shows your design’s exact size, placement, and colors.

- **Artwork Placement:** The precise [location of your logo on the shirt](https://nwcustomapparel.net/logo-placement-shirts/), hat, or jacket (e.g., "Left Chest" or "Full Back").
- **Design Size:** The exact measurements of your logo in inches.
- **Colors and Pantone Codes:** The specific thread or [ink colors](https://nwcustomapparel.net/specialty-screen-printing-inks-effects/) that will be used, often listed with a Pantone Matching System (PMS) code to ensure color accuracy.
- **A High-Quality Mock-up:** A digital image showing your logo on the specific apparel you ordered. This is a must-have for every order.

If a company can't provide a professional **[custom apparel mock-up](https://nwcustomapparel.net/services/artwork/)**, that should be a major red flag. It suggests a lack of a legitimate art department and a shortcut in their quality control process.

## The Critical Details You Must Check on Your Approvals

You've received your documents—now what? Don't just glance at them and hit “approve.” Take a few minutes to be a detective. Your diligence here will save you time, money, and a lot of headaches.

- **Verify the Spelling:** This is a surprisingly common mistake. When you’ve seen your company’s logo a thousand times, your brain can fill in the blanks. Carefully read every word on the art approval. Sometimes, an artist might have accidentally misspelled a word when recreating a logo.
- **Confirm Design Placement:** Look at the mock-up and confirm that the logo is in the exact spot you want it. Is it centered? Is it at the correct height? What looks great in a small thumbnail might be too high or too low on the actual garment.
- **Check Colors:** If your company has a specific brand color, make sure the [Pantone code](https://nwcustomapparel.net/pantone-vs-cmyk-vs-rgb-apparel-printing/) on the approval document matches your brand guidelines.
- **Review All Sizes and Quantities:** Don't skim this part. Tally up the numbers to make sure the total count is correct and that the breakdown of sizes is what you intended to order.

## Real-World Examples of What Can Go Wrong

A good approval process is a shield against real-world problems. Without it, mistakes can happen that are costly and time-consuming to fix.

- A customer once approved an art file that misspelled their company's name. Because they didn’t take the extra minute to review it, an entire order of 100 shirts was produced incorrectly. Without a signed approval, the company would have been on the hook for the entire cost. With the signed document, the responsibility was clear, saving the custom apparel company from a huge loss and providing a clear path to re-ordering.
- A company ordered custom jackets but failed to specify the logo placement. The production team, working with a standard placement, put the logo on the left chest. The customer expected it on the sleeve. Because no mock-up or art approval was signed, a dispute arose. This could have been completely avoided with a simple, signed art approval.
