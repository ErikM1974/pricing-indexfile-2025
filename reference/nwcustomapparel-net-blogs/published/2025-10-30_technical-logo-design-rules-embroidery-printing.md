# Technical Logo Design Rules for Embroidery and Screen Printing

## Why Technical Rules Matter More Than Aesthetics

Your designer created a beautiful logo. It looks perfect on a website header and a business card. Then you send it to us for embroidery on 100 polos and we have to reject it.

The text is too small to stitch. The gradients cannot be reproduced in thread. The fine lines blur into solid shapes when reduced to left-chest size. What works on a screen fails on fabric because fabric has physical limits that pixels do not.

At Northwest Custom Apparel in Milton, Washington, we have transformed thousands of logos into production-ready decoration files since 1977. We have learned which designs survive the transition and which ones require expensive rework. This article covers the five technical rules that determine whether your logo will embroider cleanly, screen print sharply, and look professional on every garment.

## Rule 1. Design for the Smallest Canvas First

The standard left-chest embroidery area is 3.5 inches wide. Your logo must work at that size before it works at any larger size. This is where most designs fail.

### Embroidery Minimums

Our Tajima TMAR-KC embroidery machine uses needles that are 0.3 millimeters wide. Thread is thicker than a pixel. A line that looks crisp on screen becomes a single stitch on fabric.

![converse_logo](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2017/01/converse_logo-300x185-1.webp)

- **Text height:** Minimum 0.25 inches (6.4 mm). Smaller text merges into illegible blobs. We call this "sewing shut."
- **Line weight:** Minimum 0.04 inches (1 mm). Thinner lines disappear into the fabric texture.
- **Letter spacing:** Moderate to wide. Tight spacing causes letters to merge during stitching.

Before finalizing your logo, reduce it to 3.5 inches wide on your screen. If you cannot read every letter clearly, the embroidery machine cannot read it either.

### Screen Print Minimums

Screen printing pushes ink through a mesh screen. Fine detail does not transfer cleanly.

- **Line weight:** Minimum 2 points (approximately 0.028 inches). Thinner lines break or blur.
- **Reverse print (light on dark):** Minimum 3 points. Light ink on dark fabric requires more ink deposit, which spreads slightly.
- **Halftone dots:** Minimum 30 lines per inch for apparel. Smaller dots clog the mesh.

Test your logo at the size you intend to print. A full-back design at 12 inches wide has more tolerance than a left-chest design at 3.5 inches. Test both.

### Vector Format Requirement

We require vector files in AI, EPS, or PDF format. Raster files (JPEG, PNG) cannot scale without degradation and cannot be digitized for embroidery. Your designer should provide a fully vectorized version with fonts outlined and gradients simplified.

If your logo exists only as a raster image, our [logo digitizing for embroidery](https://nwcustomapparel.net/logo-digitizing-for-embroidery/) service can recreate it as a vector file, but this adds cost and time. Provide vector files from the start.

## Rule 2. Limit Color Count for Cost Control

Every color in your logo adds production complexity and cost. This is not a suggestion. It is a physical constraint of the equipment.

### Screen Printing Color Economics

Each color requires a separate screen, setup, and production pass.

| Color Count | Screens Required | Relative Cost | Best For |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 color | 1 screen | Baseline | Simple logos, bulk orders |
| 2 colors | 2 screens | +40% | Standard designs |
| 3 colors | 3 screens | +70% | Complex designs |
| 4–6 colors | 4–6 screens | +100–200% | Specialty designs |
| 7+ colors | Not recommended | Prohibitive | Use DTG instead |

The sweet spot for cost-effective screen printing is 1 to 3 colors. Beyond 6 colors, production becomes specialized and expensive. If your brand requires 7 or more colors, use [direct-to-garment printing](https://nwcustomapparel.net/direct-to-garment/) for small orders or simplify the design for screen print.

### Embroidery Thread Matching

Embroidery uses finite thread colors. We carry approximately 250 thread colors from Madeira and Isacord libraries. Your designer must confirm that brand colors can be matched to available threads.

- **Pantone matching:** Specify Pantone Solid Coated codes. We match to the closest thread color. Exact matches are not always possible.
- **RGB/HEX conversion:** Digital colors look different on fabric. Never rely on screen colors for thread selection.
- **Gradient elimination:** Embroidery cannot reproduce gradients. They must be converted to solid color bands or shading stitches.

If your brand identity allows, consolidate similar shades. Merging light and dark gray into a single gray reduces thread changes without losing recognition.

## Rule 3. Simplify Gradients and Effects

Complex gradients, soft shadows, and photographic detail do not translate to thread or standard screen printing ink.

### Gradient Handling by Method

| Method | Gradient Capability | Required Simplification |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Embroidery | None | Convert to solid color bands or shading stitches |
| Screen printing | Limited (halftone dots) | Simplify to 2–3 solid color steps |
| DTG printing | Full | No simplification needed |

A logo with a blue gradient fade from navy to sky blue requires three solid blue steps for embroidery. That adds thread changes and cost. For screen printing, it requires halftone dot patterns that are difficult to hold on fabric texture.

![Split graphic contrasting a smooth, photographic gradient (labeled 'DTG Ready') with the same area converted into distinct, visible color bands/steps (labeled 'Embroidery/Screen Print Ready').](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/gradient-conversion-dtg-vs-screenprint-1024x559.webp)

Gradient Reality: When Gradients Must Become Solid Steps

Our recommendation: design a primary version with solid colors for embroidery and screen print. Create a full-gradient version for digital applications. Provide both to your decorator.

### Drop Shadows and 3D Effects

Drop shadows add dimension on screens. On fabric, they add color count and complexity without adding clarity. A drop shadow on an embroidered logo requires a second thread color and additional stitch density. The result is rarely visible from a normal viewing distance.

Remove drop shadows for apparel versions. If depth is essential, use outline weight or placement variation (offset duplicate) instead.

## Rule 4. Design for the Fabric, Not Just the Logo

A logo that works on a smooth T-shirt may fail on a textured polo. The fabric determines how the decoration sits and how the design reads.

### Fabric Texture and Decoration Density

| Fabric Type | Texture | Decoration Risk | Design Adjustment |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Pique polo | Waffle texture | Solid fills sink into valleys | Use open shapes, less fill |
| Linen | Loose weave | Embroidery distorts weave | Light stitch density, heavy backing |
| Fine Pima cotton | Smooth, thin | Puckering from dense stitches | Reduce stitch count, add stabilizer |
| Heavy cotton twill | Tight, thick | Minimal risk | Standard design rules apply |
| Polyester performance | Smooth, synthetic | Dye migration with screen print | Use low-bleed ink or DTF |

Large, dense embroidered areas pull thin fabric inward. This [causes puckering and distortion](https://www.qualitylogoproducts.com/blog/embroidery-pucker-prevent/). We prevent this by adjusting stitch density and adding specialized backing, but a simplified logo makes the job easier and the result cleaner.

### Open Space Is Your Friend

Designs with breathing room perform better on fabric than dense, filled shapes. Open space reduces stitch count, minimizes distortion, and improves readability at small sizes.

A logo with a solid circle background requires 8,000 stitches. The same logo with a circle outline requires 2,000 stitches. The outline version is flatter, cleaner, and costs less to produce.

## Rule 5. Plan for Reverse-Out on Dark Garments

Your logo must function on both light and dark backgrounds. Most businesses need white or light-colored versions for navy, black, and charcoal garments.

### Screen Printing on Dark Fabric

Printing light ink on dark fabric requires an underbase (a layer of white ink beneath the final color). This adds a production step and increases ink thickness.

- **Simplified reverse-out versions:** Reduce detail in the light-on-dark version. Remove fine lines that will fill in with the thicker ink deposit.
- **Registration tolerance:** Multi-color reverse prints require precise alignment between the underbase and top colors. Minimize contact points between colors to reduce misalignment risk.

### Embroidery on Dark Fabric

Embroidery does not require an underbase. Thread color sits on top of the fabric. However, light thread on dark fabric needs higher stitch density for coverage. This increases cost slightly but produces a cleaner result than screen printing on dark garments.

Our recommendation: create a primary full-color version for light garments and a simplified reverse-out version for dark garments. Provide both to your decorator.

## The Production-Ready Checklist

Before sending your logo to us, verify:

- [ ] Vector file in AI, EPS, or PDF format with fonts outlined
- [ ] Tested at 3.5 inches wide for left-chest embroidery
- [ ] Text no smaller than 0.25 inches (6.4 mm)
- [ ] Lines no thinner than 2 points for screen print, 0.04 inches for embroidery
- [ ] Color count limited to 3 for standard screen print, 6 maximum
- [ ] Pantone Solid Coated codes specified for each color
- [ ] Gradients simplified to solid steps for embroidery and screen print
- [ ] Reverse-out version created for dark garments
- [ ] Open space prioritized over dense fills

If you meet these requirements, your logo will embroider cleanly, screen print sharply, and look professional on every garment.
