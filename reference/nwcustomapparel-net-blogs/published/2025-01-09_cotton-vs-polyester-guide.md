# Cotton vs Polyester for Custom Apparel: What We See in Production

We get this question at least once a week. A buyer has found the garment they want, they know the quantity, and they are ready to order. Then they pause and ask: does the fabric actually matter, or is it just about feel?

It matters a lot, but not for the reasons most fabric guides focus on. We are not here to talk about breathability for afternoon jogs or which fabric is better for sensitive skin. We are a custom apparel shop in Milton, Washington. We run embroidery on Tajima machines, screen printing, DTG, and DTF every day. We have done this since 1977. What we care about is how the fabric you choose affects your logo quality, your print durability, and how the garment holds up after the first 50 washes.

This guide gives you our production-based read on cotton, polyester, and blends — organized by what matters to a business buyer placing a real order.

## What Cotton Does Well for Custom Branded Apparel

Cotton is a natural fiber with an open structure. That openness is its biggest advantage for custom decoration.

When we run [screen printing](https://nwcustomapparel.net/services/screen-printing/) on 100% cotton, plastisol ink absorbs into the fiber rather than sitting on top of it. The result is color that looks saturated and holds definition clearly. Bright colors on white cotton shirts consistently outperform the same colors on blends in terms of initial vibrancy.

For DTG printing, cotton is the standard. The ink bonds with the fiber directly. We do not need a white underbase on light-colored cotton garments the way we do on polyester. A 100% ringspun cotton shirt — Bella+Canvas Airlume, for example — gives us the sharpest DTG output we produce.

For embroidery, cotton grips the thread naturally. The fiber structure creates friction against the stitch, which holds it in place. That grip produces clean edges on logos.

The limitation with cotton is shrinkage. If the garment is not pre-shrunk before we embroider it, the fabric contracts after the first wash and pulls the stitches tight. Puckering follows. This is why we always specify pre-shrunk cotton for embroidered orders and why we ask about wash conditions before recommending a garment spec for workwear programs.

## What Polyester Does Well for Custom Branded Apparel

Polyester is a synthetic fiber with a tight, smooth surface. Its advantages are almost the opposite of cotton's.

Polyester holds its shape. It resists shrinkage. It does not fade as quickly across repeated washes. For workwear that goes through commercial laundry weekly, polyester and poly-cotton blends outlast 100% cotton in color consistency and dimensional stability. A construction crew running 65/35 polyester-cotton shirts through an industrial wash cycle every few days will see less degradation than the same crew running 100% cotton.

For [DTF printing](https://nwcustomapparel.net/services/direct-to-film-printing/), polyester is excellent. DTF transfers bond to synthetic fibers cleanly without pretreatment. If a customer already has a specific polyester performance garment they want to brand — safety vests, athletic polos, moisture-wicking zip-ups — DTF handles it with full color and no fabric restrictions.

The limitation with polyester for printing is dye migration. When we run screen printing on 100% polyester with standard plastisol ink, the heat from curing can cause the polyester dye to bleed into the ink layer. The print takes on a faint ghost of the fabric color underneath. To avoid this, we use low-cure inks or water-based inks on 100% polyester screen print orders. For DTG on polyester, we apply a pretreatment solution and lay down a white underbase before the color layers. The extra steps add cost. That is why most bulk screen print and DTG orders default to cotton or a cotton-dominant blend rather than 100% polyester.

## Why Most Business Orders Use a Cotton-Polyester Blend

For the majority of corporate programs, uniform orders, and promo shirt runs, a cotton-polyester blend is the practical answer. It gives us a surface that accepts ink and holds embroidery stitches without the shrinkage risk of 100% cotton and without the dye migration risk of 100% polyester.

The three blends we work with most often are:

**65/35 polyester-cotton:** Our standard recommendation for workwear, [corporate uniform programs](https://nwcustomapparel.net/services/staff-corporate-uniforms/), and any embroidered order that will go through frequent commercial washing. The polyester content keeps the garment dimensionally stable through wash cycles. The cotton content provides stitch grip for embroidery and ink absorption for printing. This blend survives industrial laundering better than either fiber alone.

**50/50 cotton-polyester:** Our go-to for bulk event shirts, promo tees, and screen-printed orders where cost and durability both matter. It is widely available, affordable at scale, and prints reliably with standard plastisol inks. Colors are slightly less vibrant than 100% cotton but the shirts last longer.

**Tri-blends (cotton/polyester/rayon):** These add softness and drape. They are popular for retail-style custom shirts and fashion-forward team apparel. We use tri-blends on DTG orders where soft hand feel is a priority. The rayon content creates a slightly uneven ink absorption surface, which is useful for vintage or worn-look effects but is not ideal for crisp, clean logo reproduction.

## How Fabric Choice Affects Each Decoration Method

This is where the decision gets specific. Different fabrics behave differently under each printing method. Here is what we observe in production.

### Embroidery

Cotton-dominant fabrics provide better stitch grip than 100% polyester. Our preference for embroidered workwear is the 65/35 polyester-cotton blend. The cotton grips the thread. The polyester prevents post-wash shrinkage from pulling the logo tight. On 100% polyester, we adjust pull compensation to compensate for the slick surface and reduce stitch density on large fill areas to avoid thread shift.

On unshrunk 100% cotton, the first commercial wash is the risk point. The fabric contracts. If it was not pre-shrunk at the mill, the stitches tighten and the logo puckers. We specify pre-shrunk garments for all cotton embroidery orders because of this.

For a full breakdown of fabric performance by garment type — including GSM specifications, stabilizer selection, and wash durability data — read our [best fabrics for embroidery guide](https://nwcustomapparel.net/best-fabrics-for-embroidery/).

### Screen Printing

Cotton is the standard substrate for screen printing with plastisol ink. The fiber absorbs the ink. Colors cure at the correct temperature without risk of dye migration. On a 100% white cotton shirt, our screen-printed colors match proof expectations consistently.

On 50/50 blends, ink adhesion is reliable and the garments last longer through washing. Colors are slightly less saturated than on 100% cotton but the tradeoff is worth it for most bulk orders.

On 100% polyester, we use low-cure or water-based inks to prevent dye migration. The job costs more because of the ink type and extended cure process. If a customer needs polyester and wants large multi-color prints, we often recommend DTF instead of screen printing because DTF handles the polyester surface without the migration risk and without the per-color setup cost.

### DTG Printing

One hundred percent ringspun cotton is our preferred substrate for [DTG printing](https://nwcustomapparel.net/direct-to-garment/). The ink bonds to the cotton fiber directly. On light garments, we print in a single pass. The output is sharp, vibrant, and soft to the touch after washing.

Blends above 50% cotton work for DTG. Below that threshold, ink adhesion drops and colors lose accuracy. On dark blended garments, we always apply a white underbase regardless of the cotton content.

On 100% polyester, DTG requires specialized pretreatment and a white underbase on every garment, dark or light. Dye migration remains a risk even with pretreatment on some polyester dye lots. For polyester garments with complex full-color artwork, DTF is a more reliable and often lower-cost option. Read our [DTG printing on different fabrics guide](https://nwcustomapparel.net/dtg-printing-on-different-fabrics-best-practices/) for the full pretreatment process.

### DTF Printing

DTF is the one decoration method where fabric content does not limit us. Direct-to-film transfers bond to cotton, polyester, blends, nylon, canvas, and spandex. We do not need pretreatment. We do not adjust the process based on fiber content.

![polyester safety vest with a DTF print](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/IMG_9013-1024x763.jpg)

This makes DTF our default recommendation when a customer has already committed to a specific polyester garment and wants full-color printing — especially for performance tops, safety vests, and branded athletic gear. The transfer lays on top of the fabric surface rather than bonding with the fiber, so dye migration is not a factor. Colors stay accurate regardless of the underlying fabric dye.

## Fabric Decision by Use Case

If you know your use case, the fabric decision usually follows.

| Use Case | Recommended Fabric | Decoration Method |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Construction and trades workwear | 65/35 polyester-cotton twill | Custom embroidery or DTF |
| Corporate polo program | 60/40 cotton-poly pique knit | Custom embroidery |
| Event and promo t-shirts | 50/50 cotton-polyester | Screen printing |
| Small-run custom shirts | 100% ringspun cotton | DTG printing |
| Athletic and performance gear | 100% polyester | DTF printing |
| School team uniforms | 50/50 or 65/35 blend | Screen printing or embroidery |

For [custom t-shirt printing](https://nwcustomapparel.net/services/custom-t-shirt-printing/) in bulk, we typically steer buyers toward 50/50 cotton-poly. For workwear with an embroidered logo that will survive job site conditions and weekly washing, the 65/35 blend in a twill or pique knit is our consistent recommendation.

## The One Question That Simplifies the Decision

If you are not sure where to start, answer this: what decoration method are you planning?

Embroidery? Specify pre-shrunk cotton or a 65/35 cotton-poly blend. Avoid 100% polyester on dense logo placements.

Screen printing? Specify 100% cotton or a 50/50 blend for standard plastisol. Avoid 100% polyester unless you want low-cure inks.

DTG printing? Specify 100% ringspun cotton. Anything below 50% cotton will produce weaker color.

DTF printing? Specify whatever fabric suits your use case. DTF works on all of them.

That one question cuts through most fabric decisions. If you need help choosing the fabric before choosing the garment, our team at NW Custom Apparel walks through this at the start of every order. We would rather get the spec right before production than deal with a reorder after.

## FAQs

**Which fabric is better for embroidered work uniforms, cotton or polyester?**

For embroidered workwear that goes through frequent commercial washing, we recommend a 65/35 polyester-cotton blend. The polyester content resists shrinkage, which prevents the fabric from contracting around the stitches after washing. The cotton content provides the stitch grip that keeps logo edges clean. If you are specifying 100% cotton, always confirm it is pre-shrunk. Unshrunk cotton embroidery puckers after the first industrial wash cycle.

**Can you screen print on 100% polyester?**

Yes, but it requires a different ink system. Standard plastisol inks cure at temperatures that cause polyester dye to migrate into the ink layer. We use low-cure or water-based inks on 100% polyester screen print jobs to prevent this. The process costs more than printing on cotton or a blend. For polyester garments with full-color or multi-color designs, DTF is often a better fit because it handles polyester without the migration risk and without the per-color setup cost.

**What fabric do you recommend for bulk event t-shirts?**

A 50/50 cotton-polyester blend is our standard recommendation for bulk event shirts. It prints reliably with standard screen printing inks, holds color through repeated washing, and costs less than premium 100% ringspun cotton at volume. If the shirts are going to be DTG-printed with a complex design, we upgrade to 100% ringspun cotton for better ink absorption.

**Why does my embroidered logo pucker after washing?**

Puckering after washing is almost always a fabric shrinkage issue. If the garment fabric was not pre-shrunk before embroidery, the first wash causes the fabric to contract. The stitches cannot contract with it. The fabric tightens around them and the logo distorts. On your next order, specify pre-shrunk garments, or move to a 65/35 polyester-cotton blend, which resists shrinkage regardless of wash temperature.

**What is the difference between a 50/50 and 65/35 blend?**

The ratio refers to the proportion of polyester to cotton. A 50/50 blend is half cotton, half polyester. It is softer and absorbs ink slightly better than a 65/35. A 65/35 blend is 65% polyester and 35% cotton. It is more durable, more resistant to shrinkage, and holds up better through commercial washing. For embroidered workwear and uniform programs, we default to 65/35. For event and promo shirts, we default to 50/50.
