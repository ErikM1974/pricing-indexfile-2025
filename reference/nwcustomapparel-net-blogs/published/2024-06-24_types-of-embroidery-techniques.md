## What Are the Main Commercial Embroidery Techniques?

Commercial embroidery falls into five categories. Each technique produces a different visual effect, costs a different amount, and suits different applications. We will walk through each one so you can make an informed decision before requesting a quote.

### Flat Embroidery for Corporate Apparel and Workwear

Flat embroidery is the most common commercial technique. We stitch your logo directly onto the fabric surface, creating a smooth two-dimensional finish. This method accounts for more than 80 percent of our orders.

Flat embroidery works best for detailed logos, small text, and multi-color designs. It performs well on cotton, polyester, pique, denim, and canvas. The three stitch types we use in flat embroidery are satin stitch for lettering and outlines, fill stitch (also called tatami) for large areas, and running stitch for small details.

Pricing starts at $0.50 to $1.50 per 1,000 stitches, depending on quantity. A typical left chest logo uses 5,000 to 8,000 stitches. Flat embroidery lasts 50 or more washes because thread is stitched into the fabric. It does not crack or peel like printed ink.

We recommend flat embroidery for corporate polo shirts, work jackets, uniforms, and any garment that sees frequent washing. It provides the best balance of detail, durability, and cost for most business applications.

### 3D Puff Embroidery for Bold Branding on Caps and Jackets

3D puff embroidery creates a raised, tactile effect using foam underlay beneath the stitches. The foam pushes the thread upward, giving your logo a three-dimensional appearance that stands out from the fabric surface.

This technique works best for bold block letters, simple shapes, and designs with thick elements. It does not work well for thin lines, small text under 0.2 inches, or intricate details. The foam thickness ranges from 2 to 3 millimeters. We cap the foam with a dense satin stitch to create smooth edges.

3D puff costs 30 to 50 percent more than flat embroidery because of the additional material and labor. A cap front panel in 3D puff typically uses 8,000 to 15,000 stitches. Durability is lower than flat embroidery. The foam compresses over time, especially with machine drying. We recommend inside-out washing and air drying for 3D puff garments.

We recommend 3D puff for baseball caps, streetwear, and outerwear where bold branding matters more than long-term wash durability. It creates visual impact that flat embroidery cannot match.

### Applique Embroidery for Sports Teams and Varsity Jackets

Applique embroidery sews fabric pieces onto a base garment, then borders them with satin stitch. The result is a layered, textured look that reduces overall stitch count compared to all-stitch designs.

This technique works best for large logos, sports team names, varsity jackets, and cheerleading uniforms. Common materials include twill, felt, and cotton patches. We cut the fabric to shape, position it on the garment, and stitch the border.

Applique reduces stitch count, which lowers cost for large designs. However, it adds fabric and labor costs. Turnaround time runs slightly longer because of material preparation. A typical varsity jacket back in applique uses 15,000 to 25,000 stitches plus fabric pieces.

We recommend applique for school sports teams, motorcycle clubs, and organizations wanting a classic athletic look. It provides visual weight and texture that all-stitch embroidery cannot achieve at the same size.

### Textured Embroidery for Boutique and Specialty Applications

Textured embroidery uses specialized stitches like bullion knots, French knots, chain stitches, and metallic threads. These techniques create decorative, tactile surfaces that go beyond standard commercial embroidery.

This technique works best for boutique fashion brands, luxury corporate gifts, and special event apparel. It is not standard for corporate workwear or uniforms because of the higher cost and limited availability. Most commercial embroidery machines cannot produce these stitches without specialized attachments.

Textured embroidery costs two to three times more than flat embroidery because of the slow production speed and skilled labor required. We recommend it for limited-run projects where visual distinction justifies the premium.

### Digitized Embroidery: The Process Behind Every Order

Digitized embroidery is not a decorative technique. It is the process that converts your artwork into a machine-readable stitch file. Every embroidery order requires digitizing, whether the final result is flat, 3D puff, or applique.

We use Wilcom and Hatch software to import your vector file, map stitches, set density, add underlay, and sequence colors. The digitizer makes decisions that affect quality, durability, and cost. Poor digitizing causes thread breaks, fabric puckering, and misaligned colors.

Digitizing costs $29.99 to $45 as a one-time fee per design. We save your file permanently for reorders. You never pay digitizing again for the same logo. Reorders using a saved file ship faster because we skip the setup phase.

We recommend submitting vector files in AI, EPS, or PDF format. Raster files in PNG or JPG work at 300 DPI minimum, but vector files produce cleaner digitizing results.

## What Stitch Types Do Commercial Embroidery Machines Use?

Commercial embroidery machines produce three main stitch types. Each serves a different purpose in your design.

Satin stitch creates smooth, lustrous lines with a density of 0.3 to 0.4 millimeters. We use it for lettering, outlines, and borders. It produces the polished look you see on corporate polo shirts and left chest logos.

Fill stitch, also called tatami stitch, creates dense textured coverage for large areas. It lays down an underlay foundation first, then covers it with overlapping rows of thread. We use fill stitch for full back designs, large patches, and backgrounds.

Running stitch produces a dashed, single-line effect. We use it for small details, fine lines, and accents under 0.05 inches. It adds detail without adding significant stitch count or cost.

## Embroidery vs Screen Printing: Which Lasts Longer on Workwear?

Embroidery stitches thread into fabric. Screen printing pushes ink onto the surface. This fundamental difference affects durability, cost, and suitability for workwear.

Flat embroidery lasts 50 or more washes. The thread is part of the garment. It does not crack, peel, or fade. Screen printing lasts 30 to 50 washes. The ink sits on top of the fabric and may crack on stretch or degrade in industrial laundry.

Embroidery costs more upfront because of digitizing fees and slower production. Screen printing costs less for large designs but requires setup for each color. Over the life of the garment, embroidery often costs less per wear because it outlasts screen printing two to one.

For workwear in Washington state, embroidery holds up better in our climate. The Pacific Northwest receives 37 inches of annual rainfall. Thread resists moisture better than printed ink, which can degrade faster in wet conditions. We recommend embroidery for outerwear, work jackets, and uniforms that face daily wear and frequent washing.

Screen printing works better for large full-back designs, event t-shirts, and cotton garments where cost matters more than longevity. Many of our clients use a hybrid approach: embroidery on the left chest for durability and screen printing on the back for large graphics.

## How Do You Choose the Right Stabilizer for Your Embroidery Project?

Stabilizer is the backing material that supports your fabric during stitching. The wrong stabilizer causes puckering, distortion, or complete design failure. Use this decision table to choose correctly.

| Fabric Type | Stabilizer Type | Why | Common Examples |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Knits, t-shirts, polos | Cutaway | Prevents stretch and distortion | Sport-Tek, Port Authority, Gildan |
| Woven cotton, denim, canvas | Tearaway | Stable fabric, easy removal | Carhartt, Dri Duck, CornerStone |
| Towels, fleece, terrycloth | Tearaway plus water-soluble topper | Holds nap down for crisp edges | Beach towels, hoodies, bathrobes |
| Sheer fabrics, organza | Washaway | Dissolves completely, no visible backing | Bridal, decorative, dancewear |
| Stretchy performance wear | Cutaway plus lightweight woven | Prevents halo effect, supports stretch | Athletic polos, compression gear |

Cutaway stabilizer stays with the garment permanently. We use it for stretchy fabrics like knits and performance wear because it prevents distortion through hundreds of washes. Tearaway stabilizer removes cleanly after stitching. We use it for stable woven fabrics like denim and canvas.

Water-soluble topper sits on top of fluffy fabrics like fleece and towels. It holds the fibers down so stitches land cleanly. Without it, the thread sinks into the fabric and loses definition. Washaway stabilizer dissolves in water. We use it for sheer fabrics where any visible backing would ruin the appearance.

## Polyester Thread vs Rayon Thread: Which Should Your Order Use?

Thread type affects appearance, durability, and cost. We offer both polyester and rayon thread. Here is how they compare.

| Factor | Polyester Thread | Rayon Thread (Viscose) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Appearance | Strong, straightforward shine | Silky, subtle sheen |
| Durability | High, bleach resistant, colorfast | Moderate, may fade with bleach |
| Feel | Slightly stiffer, harder touch | Soft, flexible, skin-friendly |
| Best for | Industrial laundries, sports uniforms, workwear | Corporate apparel, retail brands, fashion |
| Color range | Standard palette | 400 or more shades, better color matching |
| Cost | Lower | Slightly higher |

We default to polyester thread for workwear orders. It withstands industrial laundry, bleach, and high heat without fading. Construction companies, healthcare facilities, and food service operations benefit from polyester's durability.

We recommend rayon thread for corporate polos, retail brands, and fashion apparel where a softer sheen matters more than industrial wash resistance. Rayon's 400-plus shade range provides better color matching for brand-specific Pantone colors.

Metallic thread adds gold or silver sparkle for special events and decorative applications. It requires slower machine speed and careful handling to prevent thread breaks.

## How Do You Prepare Artwork for Embroidery Digitizing?

Proper artwork preparation saves time, reduces digitizing costs, and produces better results. Follow this checklist before submitting your file.

1. Submit vector files in AI, EPS, or PDF format when possible. Vector artwork scales without losing resolution.
2. Minimum text height is 0.25 inches or 6.35 millimeters. Smaller text becomes illegible when stitched.
3. Minimum line thickness is 0.05 inches or 1.27 millimeters. Thinner lines break or disappear.
4. Limit your design to six or fewer solid colors. Thread cannot blend like ink. Gradients do not translate to embroidery.
5. Remove thin outlines and small details under 0.05 inches. These do not reproduce well at stitch scale.
6. Convert all fonts to outlines before sending. This prevents font substitution errors.
7. Include Pantone color references if brand color accuracy matters. We match to the closest thread shade.
8. Raster files in PNG or JPG format work at 300 DPI minimum. Low-resolution files require recreation, which adds cost.

Common mistakes we see include JPG files with low resolution, gradient fills that cannot translate to thread, tiny text under 0.25 inches that stitches as illegible blobs, and complex photorealistic images that embroidery cannot reproduce. Simplify your design for the best embroidery results.

## How Durable Is Embroidery Compared to Other Decoration Methods?

Durability varies by technique and decoration method. Here is what we have observed over 48 years of production.

Flat embroidery maintains thread integrity through 50 or more washes. The thread is stitched into the fabric structure. It does not crack, peel, or fade under normal conditions. Polyester thread withstands industrial laundry, bleach, and high heat.

3D puff embroidery shows foam compression after 30 to 40 washes. The raised effect diminishes over time. Air drying extends lifespan compared to machine drying.

Screen printing lasts 30 to 50 washes. Ink may crack on stretch fabrics or degrade in industrial laundry. Water-based inks fade faster than plastisol.

DTG printing lasts 20 to 30 washes. The water-based ink fades faster than embroidery thread, especially on garments exposed to frequent washing and Pacific Northwest rain.

For workwear in Washington state, we recommend embroidery for longevity. The 37 inches of annual rainfall in the Pacific Northwest accelerates ink degradation on printed garments. Thread resists moisture better and maintains appearance through years of use.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Commercial Embroidery

**Can you embroider on any fabric?**

Most fabrics accept embroidery. Cotton, polyester, pique, denim, and canvas produce the best results. Silk, rayon, and very thin knits are challenging and require specialized stabilizers. Leather needs special needles. Waterproof fabrics are not suitable because stitching punctures the seal.

**Can embroidery be removed if we rebrand?**

Removal is possible with a seam ripper but leaves visible holes in the fabric. Cover-up embroidery stitches a new design over the old one, which adds bulk and may show through lightweight fabrics. We recommend replacing the garment for professional results.

**What file format do I need for embroidery?**

Vector files in AI, EPS, or PDF format produce the best digitizing results. We also accept raster files in PNG or JPG at 300 DPI minimum. Your digitizer creates the final production file in DST, PES, or EXP format.

**How many colors can my embroidered logo have?**

Our commercial machines hold 15 thread colors. The practical limit for cost efficiency is 6 colors. Each color change adds machine time. Unlimited color embroidery exists as a specialty service but costs significantly more.

**Is embroidery better than screen printing for uniforms?**

Yes for longevity. Embroidery outlasts screen printing two to one on workwear. No for large designs. Screen printing costs less for full-back graphics. Many of our clients use a hybrid approach: embroidery on the left chest and screen printing on the back.

**Do you offer rush embroidery for events?**

Yes. Rush embroidery ships in 2 to 3 business days with a 25 percent surcharge. Availability depends on our production schedule. We recommend ordering 2 weeks before your event to avoid rush fees and ensure the best results.
