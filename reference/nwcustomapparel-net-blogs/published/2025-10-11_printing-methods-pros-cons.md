# T-Shirt and Polo Shirt Printing Methods: Pros, Cons, and Which One Fits Your Order

The right printing method is not the one with the best marketing. It is the one that matches your garment, your quantity, and your design. Choosing wrong costs money and produces results that disappoint — a cracked screen print after 20 washes, an embroidered logo too small to read, a DTG print that looks pale on a navy hoodie.

This guide covers every major decoration method used on T-shirts, polo shirts, hoodies, caps, and athletic apparel. For each method, you get how it works, what it does well, where it fails, and which garments and buyer situations it actually suits. The [Printing United Alliance](https://www.printing.org/), the global trade association for the printing and graphic arts industries, states that decoration method selection should consider garment type, wash frequency, and intended use. That is the framework this article uses.

## 7 Main T-Shirt and Apparel Printing Methods

The decoration methods used on custom apparel fall into three categories: digital printing methods (DTG, DTF, dye sublimation), analog printing methods (screen printing, discharge printing), specialty transfer methods (heat transfer vinyl), and stitching methods (embroidery). Understanding where each one fits (by garment, by quantity, by design type) makes the decision straightforward.

### Screen Printing

Screen printing pushes ink through a mesh stencil onto fabric. One screen per color. After all colors are applied, the garment passes through a heat conveyor that cures the ink permanently onto the fabric surface.

![Screen Printing Art Work](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2016/12/21772-Ultimate-Guys-Weekend-2013-FP-2-232x300-1.webp)

The industry standard ink for garment screen printing is plastisol: [PVC particles suspended in a liquid plasticizer](https://mutshippingcustoms.com/blogs/news/what-is-plastisol-ink-understanding-the-basics-of-this-printing-method) that remain wet until cured at approximately 320°F. Once cured, plastisol ink bonds firmly to cotton fibers and produces vivid, opaque color.

**Pros:** Highest color vibrancy of any method on cotton. Excellent durability under home laundering — properly cured plastisol on quality cotton survives 50+ wash cycles without significant fading. Fastest method per unit once screens are made. The only method that achieves exact Pantone color matching reliably. Handles specialty inks including metallic, glow-in-the-dark, and puff inks that no other method can replicate.

**Cons:** Setup cost is significant — each color requires a separate screen burned, registered, and loaded before a single shirt runs. This makes screen printing the worst choice for small orders or designs with many colors. Plastisol ink degrades faster than embroidery thread under commercial laundering (hot water, industrial drying). Screen printing cannot be applied to curved surfaces like structured baseball caps.

**Best for:** Bulk T-shirt orders of 24 or more pieces with simple 1–6 color designs. Event shirts, school spirit wear, company all-hands T-shirts, promotional giveaways. Any garment requiring Hi-Vis ANSI certification — screen printing is the only decoration method that does not compromise retroreflective tape, per [ANSI/ISEA 107-2015](https://webstore.ansi.org/standards/isea/ansiisea1072015).

**Not for:** Orders under 12–24 pieces. Complex multicolor designs with gradients or photographic detail. Baseball caps. Polar fleece and sherpa (high-pile surface prevents clean ink transfer).

For [bulk screen printed T-shirts for teams and businesses](/bulk-screen-printed-tees-order/), screen printing at quantities above 100 pieces produces the lowest per-unit cost of any decoration method on cotton.

### Direct to Garment (DTG) Printing

DTG uses inkjet printer technology adapted for textiles. The garment is loaded into the machine, and print heads spray water-based ink directly onto the fabric surface. The ink is then cured with a heat press.

On dark garments, a pretreatment solution is applied first. The pretreatment creates a base that allows white ink to adhere and show full color without being absorbed by the dark fabric. The pretreatment process adds a slight texture to the print area on dark garments that is not present on light garments.

**Pros:** No setup fee, no minimum order. A single shirt costs the same proportionally as 10 shirts. Handles unlimited colors, photographic designs, and fine gradients that screen printing cannot reproduce. Water-based inks are softer on the environment than plastisol. The print soaks into cotton fibers and produces a soft hand feel — closer to the fabric itself than screen printing.

**Cons:** Per-piece cost does not decrease with quantity, making DTG significantly more expensive than screen printing above 24+ pieces. Best results require 100% cotton or high-cotton blends. DTG on polyester or performance fabrics produces soft, washed-out color without specialty pretreatment. Wash durability is moderate according to [Kornit Digital](https://www.kornit.com/magazine/7-types-of-shirt-printing-and-their-benefits/), DTG prints using modern water-based inks maintain color through 30–50 washes with proper care; industrial laundering accelerates fading faster than home washing.

**Best for:** Small runs under 24 pieces. One-off personalized garments. Designs with photographic detail, gradients, or full-color artwork. E-commerce sellers fulfilling individual orders without holding inventory. Organizations wanting to test a design before committing to a screen printing run.

**Not for:** Bulk orders above 50 pieces where per-unit cost matters. Polyester performance fabrics. Orders with strict Pantone color requirements (DTG uses CMYK approximation, not exact Pantone matching).

### Direct to Film (DTF) Printing

DTF prints a full-color design onto a special film using water-based inks. A thermoplastic polyurethane (TPU) adhesive powder is applied to the wet ink layer on the film, then cured. The finished film transfer is placed face-down on the garment and heat-pressed at approximately 310°F for 12–15 seconds. The film peels away, leaving the design bonded to the fabric.

The TPU powder is what makes DTF work on virtually any fabric. Unlike DTG, which relies on ink absorbing into cotton fibers, DTF adheres mechanically via the TPU layer regardless of fiber content.

**Pros:** Works on cotton, polyester, nylon, canvas, denim, blends, and performance fabrics without the fiber-content restrictions of DTG or dye sublimation. Vibrant opaque white ink allows bright color on dark garments without the complex pretreatment required by DTG. No minimum order. Handles complex multicolor designs and gradients. The transfer can be produced in advance and applied later, which suits on-demand workflows.

**Cons:** The TPU layer sits on top of the fabric, creating a slight texture that is noticeable on soft performance fabrics. Wash durability is good (40–60 cycles under home laundering) but the TPU layer is more susceptible to cracking over time under repeated commercial laundering at high heat than screen-printed plastisol or embroidery. Not suitable for all-over printing.

**Best for:** Polyester athletic jerseys, nylon jackets, canvas bags, and mixed-fabric garments where screen printing or DTG are incompatible. Complex designs on dark hoodies where DTG struggles. Small runs on fabrics that screen printing cannot handle. Buyers who need fabric-type flexibility across a mixed garment order.

**Not for:** Garments requiring the absolute softest hand feel (dye sublimation produces zero texture). Commercial laundering environments where high heat and industrial chemicals accelerate TPU degradation.

### Dye Sublimation

Dye sublimation uses heat to convert solid dye into a gas that bonds permanently with polyester fiber molecules. The design is printed onto special transfer paper, then placed against the garment and pressed at high heat. The dye converts to a gas and infuses into the polyester fibers, where it becomes part of the fabric's molecular structure.

**Pros:** Zero hand feel - the print has no texture because it is inside the fiber, not on the surface. Completely fade-resistant under washing because the dye is part of the fabric itself, not a coating on top of it. The only method that enables true all-over, edge-to-edge printing on garment panels before assembly. Excellent for performance athletic wear, swimwear, and promotional items like mugs and mousepads.

**Cons:** Works only on 100% polyester or polymer-coated substrates. Completely ineffective on cotton — the gaseous dye has nothing to bond to in natural fibers. Cannot print on dark garments because the translucent dye cannot cover dark fabric color. Small white spots can appear where the fabric creases during heat pressing on finished garments.

**Best for:** Full-color performance athletic jerseys, swimwear, polyester promotional products (mugs, mousepads, phone cases). Brands needing all-over edge-to-edge pattern coverage on sportswear. Situations where no print texture is acceptable.

**Not for:** Cotton T-shirts. Dark-colored garments. Buyers who need embroidery-level durability for professional branding on washable uniforms.

### Discharge Printing

[Discharge printing](https://nwcustomapparel.net/discharge-printing-works/) removes the original dye from cotton fabric using a chemical agent (typically zinc formaldehyde sulfoxylate or a water-based alternative), then replaces it with a new color. Instead of ink sitting on top of the fabric, discharge printing essentially re-dyes the fabric in the print area.

**Pros:** Softer hand feel than any other printing method except dye sublimation — the print area is the same thickness as the surrounding fabric because ink is not added on top. Excellent color results, particularly for lighter designs on mid-toned garments. Preferred by fashion-forward brands and apparel lines where print texture is unacceptable.

**Cons:** Works only on 100% cotton with reactive dyes — synthetic fabrics or cotton dyed with non-reactive processes will not discharge properly. Requires careful chemical process control; inconsistent results if temperature or dwell time is off. Less widely available than screen printing or DTG. White discharge ink is not possible — the method removes color but cannot add white.

**Best for:** Fashion apparel brands prioritizing soft hand feel. Vintage or distressed design aesthetics. Premium T-shirts on reactive-dyed 100% cotton blanks.

**Not for:** Polyester or blended fabrics. Buyers who need exact color replication or Pantone matching. Hi-Vis or technical performance garments.

### Heat Transfer Vinyl (HTV)

HTV involves cutting a design from a solid-colored vinyl sheet using a computer-controlled cutter, then heat-pressing the cut vinyl onto the garment. For full-color designs, printed vinyl is cut and pressed instead of solid-color sheets.

**Pros:** Produces sharp, opaque, completely solid designs. Excellent for player names and numbers on sports jerseys because each piece is individual and easy to customize per garment. Works on a wide range of fabric types. Small initial investment makes it accessible for very small shops.

**Cons:** Each design must be cut and "weeded" (excess material removed) before pressing, making it labor-intensive for complex shapes. The vinyl layer feels noticeably thicker and stiffer than screen printing or DTF on the garment. Photographic or gradient designs are not achievable with solid-color vinyl. Printed vinyl laminates can peel from edges over time. Not cost-effective for large runs.

**Best for:** Sports jerseys requiring individual names and numbers (one piece at a time). Small custom orders of 1–12 pieces with simple, bold designs. Situations where embroidery is not suitable and the design is too simple to justify DTF production.

**Not for:** Bulk orders where screen printing is significantly cheaper. Designs with gradients or photographic detail. Garments subjected to heavy commercial laundering.

### Embroidery

Embroidery is not technically a printing method — it is a stitching method. However, it belongs in any complete guide to shirt and apparel decoration because it is the correct choice for many garments where printing methods are not appropriate.

A commercial embroidery machine stitches thread directly through the fabric and a backing stabilizer placed underneath. The thread passes through the garment and locks to the backing, integrating structurally with the fabric. This is why embroidery outlasts every printing method on garments subjected to commercial laundering, outdoor use, and daily abrasion.

**Pros:** Highest wash durability of any decoration method as embroidered logos on polo shirts survive 100+ laundering cycles without fading, cracking, or peeling. Produces a raised, three-dimensional logo that communicates professional quality for corporate and client-facing staff apparel. Color count does not affect cost — up to 15 thread colors at the same per-piece price. Once digitized, reorders run with no additional setup cost.

**Cons:** Cannot reproduce photographic designs, gradients, or fine details below 1/4-inch letter height — thread has physical limits that ink does not. Large back designs become stiff and heavy when embroidered. Lightweight fabrics under 5 oz. are prone to puckering under dense embroidery. Not suitable for Hi-Vis safety shirts — embroidery hooping voids ANSI retroreflective tape certification.

**Best for:** Polo shirts for corporate and professional staff. Work jackets and duck canvas outerwear for trades and construction crews. Baseball caps (with cylinder arm cap frame). Polar fleece and sherpa outerwear where screen printing is not compatible. Any garment that will be worn repeatedly for over a year and laundered frequently.

**Not for:** T-shirts with large print designs. Lightweight performance fabrics. Hi-Vis safety garments. Designs with gradients, photographs, or complex shading.

## Which Printing Method Fits Which Garment

This is the question no other guide answers by garment type. Here is the breakdown.

### T-Shirts (Cotton and Cotton-Poly Blends)

Screen printing is the correct method for bulk cotton T-shirt orders of 24 or more pieces with simple 1–6 color designs. It delivers the lowest per-unit cost and the best Pantone color accuracy.

DTG is the correct method for orders under 24 pieces or designs with photographic detail, gradients, or unlimited color. Per-piece cost is constant regardless of quantity.

DTF handles cotton T-shirts well and also works when the order includes mixed fabrics — for example, some cotton tees and some polyester performance shirts in the same run.

Discharge printing is the correct method when softness of hand feel is the primary requirement and the garment is 100% reactive-dyed cotton.

### Polo Shirts (Pique Knit)

Embroidery is the standard for polo shirts in corporate, healthcare, and professional uniform programs. The pique knit fabric supports cutaway backing, the raised logo reads as professional at customer-facing distance, and the design survives commercial laundering over a multi-year uniform lifespan.

Screen printing on polo shirts is possible and works for promotional or event polo orders where professional appearance is secondary to cost and design complexity.

DTF on polo shirts works for small orders where embroidery minimum quantities are not met, or for designs with color complexity that exceeds embroidery's thread capability.

For [logo embroidery for corporate uniform programs](/services/logo-embroidery/) on polo shirts, the standard placement is a left-chest logo running 6,000–10,000 stitches.

### Hoodies and Sweatshirts (Fleece)

Full-back designs and large chest graphics belong in screen printing at bulk quantities. Screen printing on fleece produces bold, durable output on the smooth face of the fabric.

Left-chest logo placement on a hoodie suits embroidery — the same method used on polo shirts works on fleece with appropriate backing weight.

DTF handles complex multicolor designs on dark hoodies where screen printing setup cost at small quantities is prohibitive.

See our [custom printed hoodies](/services/screen-printing/custom-hoodies/) for screen printing on fleece garments.

### Baseball Caps

Embroidery is the only practical decoration method for structured baseball caps. The curved, rigid surface of a cap makes screen printing mesh contact impossible. Embroidery with a cylinder arm cap frame is the industry standard. See our [custom embroidered caps and hats](/services/custom-embroidery-caps/).

### Hi-Vis Safety Shirts

Screen printing is the only ANSI-certified decoration method for Hi-Vis Class 2 and Class 3 safety garments. Embroidery hooping applies mechanical pressure to retroreflective tape and risks voiding ANSI/ISEA 107-2015 certification. See our [Hi-Vis safety stripe screen printing](/services/screen-printing/custom-safety-stripes/).

### Athletic Jerseys and Performance Shirts

Dye sublimation is the method for all-over color coverage on polyester athletic jerseys — the only method that achieves edge-to-edge printing.

DTF handles partial logo application on polyester performance shirts and nylon athletic gear where dye sublimation is not needed.

Embroidery on athletic shirts interferes with moisture-wicking properties of performance polyester. Avoid it on technical athletic fabric.

## Which Printing Method Lasts the Longest

The durability hierarchy, from most to least durable under regular laundering:

1. **Embroidery** — thread structurally integrated with fabric; does not fade, crack, or peel; outlasts the garment in most cases
2. **Screen printing (plastisol)** — 50+ wash cycles under home laundering; degrades faster under commercial laundering
3. **DTF** — 40–60 wash cycles under home laundering; TPU adhesive layer is vulnerable to high-heat commercial laundering
4. **DTG** — 30–50 wash cycles under home laundering; water-based ink fades with prolonged hot water and high-heat drying
5. **Dye sublimation** — fade-resistant on polyester indefinitely under normal conditions; the dye is part of the fiber
6. **HTV** — edges begin to lift with heavy use; interior holds but perimeter adhesion weakens over time
7. **Discharge printing** — comparable to screen printing in terms of longevity since it re-dyes the fiber; durability depends on the original fabric's reactive dye quality

One variable changes this hierarchy: laundering environment. Commercial laundering (hot water, industrial detergent, high-heat drying used by uniform services and healthcare facilities) degrades all printing methods significantly faster than home laundering. Under commercial laundering, embroidery is the only method that holds through the full garment lifespan with no visible degradation.

| 10 shirts with a full-color photograph or complex artwork | DTG | No setup fee; unlimited colors; photorealistic output |
| Polo shirts for corporate or client-facing staff | Embroidery | Durability; professional appearance; commercial laundry resistance |

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the best printing method for T-shirts?**

It depends on your order size and design. Screen printing is best for bulk orders of 24+ pieces with simple 1–6 color designs — it delivers the lowest per-unit cost and the most durable result on cotton. DTG is best for small orders under 24 pieces or designs with gradients and photographic detail. DTF is best when the order includes non-cotton fabrics that screen printing and DTG cannot handle reliably.

**Which shirt printing method lasts the longest?**

Embroidery lasts the longest — thread stitched through fabric does not fade, crack, or peel. Among printing methods, screen printing with plastisol ink on cotton lasts the longest: 50+ home wash cycles before visible degradation. DTF follows at 40–60 cycles. DTG typically shows fading between 30–50 cycles. Laundering environment matters significantly — commercial laundering degrades all print methods faster than home washing.

**Which printing method is best for polo shirts?**

Embroidery. Polo shirts worn by corporate, healthcare, or professional staff need a logo that survives repeated commercial or home laundering over a 2–3 year uniform program lifespan. Embroidery thread does not degrade under washing. The raised, tactile finish also reads as more professional than a flat printed logo at close customer-facing distance.

**What printing method works on polyester shirts?**

DTF works on any polyester content without restriction. Dye sublimation works on 100% polyester and produces the best all-over color. Screen printing works on polyester using low-bleed plastisol ink to prevent dye migration from the fabric into the ink during heat cure. Standard DTG has poor adhesion on polyester above 30–40% content.

**What is discharge printing and when should I use it?**

Discharge printing uses a chemical agent to remove the original dye from cotton fabric and replace it with a new color. Unlike screen printing, which adds ink on top of the fabric, discharge printing re-dyes the fiber. The result is a print with zero surface texture — it feels identical to the surrounding unprinted fabric. Use discharge printing on 100% reactive-dyed cotton when hand feel is the primary requirement, such as premium fashion apparel or retail garments where the print area must feel as soft as the rest of the shirt.

**Can I use both embroidery and printing on the same garment?**

Yes. Many corporate uniform programs use embroidery for the left-chest logo (professional appearance, commercial laundry durability) and screen printing for a full-back design or sleeve graphic (design complexity, cost-effectiveness at scale). The methods complement each other — matching Pantone color references for thread and ink keeps branding consistent across both decorations.
