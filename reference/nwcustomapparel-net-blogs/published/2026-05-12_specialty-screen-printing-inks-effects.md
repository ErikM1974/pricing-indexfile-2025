# Specialty Screen Printing Inks & Effects: A Production Guide for Washington Apparel Orders

Standard plastisol prints a flat, opaque layer on fabric. Specialty inks modify that layer by adding dimension, texture, light reactivity, or metallic reflection. At NW Custom Apparel’s Milton, WA facility, we run specialty effects on orders ranging from 24-piece construction crew high-visibility shirts to 500-piece school spirit wear runs. Not every design benefits from a specialty effect. The wrong choice adds cost without adding value, and some effects actively destroy fine detail that standard plastisol would preserve.

This guide explains each specialty ink we offer, the technical requirements for production, the design limitations you need to know before submitting artwork, and the cost implications for your order.

For the chemistry of standard plastisol, water-based, and discharge inks, see our complete [screen printing ink types guide](/screen-printing-ink-types/).

## How Specialty Inks Work (The Technical Foundation)

### Plastisol Base + Additive = Specialty Effect

Every specialty ink starts with the same plastisol base. The difference is the additive mixed into that base before printing:

| **Effect Type** | **Additive Mechanism** | **Cure Requirement** | **Mesh Count** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Puff | Heat-activated expanding agent | 320°F standard | 86-110 |
| High Density | Viscosity builder + stacked passes | 320°F, flash between passes | 86-110 |
| Suede | Flocking agent or post-print nap treatment | 320°F + mechanical finishing | 110-156 |
| Super Glow | Phosphorescent pigment suspension | 320°F standard | 110-156 |
| UV Color Change | Photochromic dye capsules | 320°F standard | 156-200 |
| Shimmer / Metallic | Metallic flake suspension | 320°F standard | 86-110 |

**Critical production note:** Specialty additives alter plastisol viscosity. A puff ink mixture is significantly thicker than standard plastisol and requires lower mesh counts (86-110) to pass through the screen. Fine detail printed with puff on 230 mesh simply will not transfer as the ink is too viscous to pass through the thread weave.

![Infographic showing why specialty inks require lower mesh counts (86-110) for proper flow.](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/specialty-ink-mesh-logic-1024x558.webp)

Thick specialty additives require lower mesh counts (86-110) to pass through the screen without clogging.

## Texture Effects: Puff, High Density, and Suede

### Puff Ink - The Rounded, Expanded Print

Puff ink contains a heat-activated expanding agent. When the printed garment passes through the conveyor dryer at 320°F, the additive generates gas bubbles within the plastisol layer, causing it to rise above the fabric surface. The result is a soft, rounded, three-dimensional profile.

![Macro side-view comparison of rounded puff ink versus sharp-edged high density ink.](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/puff-vs-high-density-profile-1024x558.webp)

Puff ink creates soft, organic expansion, while High Density builds sharp, architectural layers.

**Technical Specifications:**

- **Best design elements:** Organic shapes, curved lines, bold logos with minimal angular geometry
- **Minimum line weight:** 3pt (anything thinner expands unevenly or loses definition)
- **Detail limitations:** Hard edges and sharp angles soften during expansion. A crisp 90-degree corner becomes rounded. Fine detail like small text, thin outlines, and intricate patterns, blurs or disappears entirely.
- **Garment compatibility:** Works on cotton and cotton-poly blends. Avoid high-polyester content; the expansion agent reacts unpredictably with synthetic fiber surfaces.
- **Wash durability:** 25-40 wash cycles before noticeable flattening. The expanded structure is mechanically fragile compared to standard plastisol.

**When to use puff:** School mascots with rounded forms, retro-style bubble lettering, playful casual designs where a soft texture adds tactile interest.

**When NOT to use puff:** Corporate logos with sharp geometry, small text, fine line art, designs requiring precise registration on multi-color prints.

### High Density Ink - The Sharp, Stacked Profile

High density ink uses a viscosity-building additive that allows multiple ink deposits to stack vertically without slumping. Rather than expanding outward like puff, high density builds upward in controlled layers, producing a square-edged, architectural profile.

![high-density-ink](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2016/12/HIgh-Density-Ink.webp)

**Technical Specifications:**

- **Best design elements:** Hard edges, angles, geometric shapes, block lettering
- **Production method:** Multiple print passes (typically 3-5) with flash curing between each pass. Each pass adds approximately 0.3-0.5mm of height.
- **Detail limitations:** Fine points and needle-thin elements collapse under their own weight during stacking. Large fill areas develop a "sink" in the center where the ink mass contracts during cure.
- **Clear high density option:** Printing without pigment produces a tonal, darkened effect on the garment — essentially a raised, glossy texture in the garment's natural color. This works well for subtle watermark-style designs on premium apparel.
- **Wash durability:** 30-50 cycles. The stacked structure is more durable than puff but can chip at edges if the print is thick enough to catch on surfaces.

**When to use high density:** Athletic block lettering, geometric corporate marks, designs where a sharp, constructed texture communicates strength or structure.

**When NOT to use high density:** Large solid fills, designs with fine detail or hairline elements, lightweight garments where added print weight affects drape.

### Suede Ink - The Flocked, Fuzzy Finish

Suede ink creates a raised print with a nap texture similar to suede leather. There are two production methods: a flocking additive suspended in plastisol that rises during cure, or a post-print mechanical finishing process that brushes the cured surface to create a nap.

**Technical Specifications:**

- **Best design elements:** Bold shapes with adequate surface area for the nap to develop
- **Minimum line weight:** 1/8 inch (3.175mm). Anything thinner lacks sufficient surface area for the fuzzy texture to form and simply looks like a failed puff print.
- **Detail limitations:** Large fills work well. Super-fine detail does not. The nap obscures edges, so precision is lost.
- **Wash durability:** 20-35 cycles. The mechanical nap is vulnerable to abrasion and pilling in industrial wash environments.

**When to use suede:** Outdoor lifestyle brands, western/cowboy aesthetic designs, premium casual wear where a tactile, organic texture aligns with brand positioning.

**When NOT to use suede:** Workwear subject to industrial laundering, designs with fine detail, corporate applications requiring crisp precision.

## Optical Effects: Glow, UV Change, and Metallic

### Super Glow (Phosphorescent) Ink

Super glow ink contains phosphorescent pigment particles that absorb light energy and re-emit it as visible light in darkness. The "super" designation refers to higher pigment load than standard glow inks, producing brighter initial glow and longer afterglow duration.

**Technical Specifications:**

- **Pigment mechanism:** Strontium aluminate-based phosphors (modern formulation) or zinc sulfide (older, dimmer formulation). NW Custom Apparel uses strontium aluminate for maximum brightness.
- **Charge requirement:** 10-30 minutes of exposure to bright light (natural or artificial) for full charge. Glow duration: 4-8 hours depending on pigment grade and charge intensity.
- **Garment restriction:** Works on light-colored garments only — white, cream, silver, light grey. On dark garments, the phosphorescent effect is invisible because the dark fabric absorbs the emitted light. The ink itself is transparent; it relies on light-colored fabric to reflect the glow back to the viewer.
- **Visual effect on light shirts:** Tonal, slightly milky appearance in daylight. The "clear" quality creates a subtle watermark effect that becomes dramatic in darkness.
- **Wash durability:** 15-25 cycles before significant brightness degradation. Phosphorescent particles are mechanically fragile and break down under abrasion and detergent exposure.

**When to use super glow:** Event staff safety shirts, concert and festival merchandise, novelty promotional items, Halloween-themed apparel, children's sleepwear (with CPSIA-compliant formulation).

**When NOT to use super glow:** Dark garments, workwear requiring long-term durability, designs where daytime visibility of the print matters.

### UV Color Change (Photochromic) Ink

UV color change ink contains photochromic dye capsules that are colorless indoors and activate to full color when exposed to ultraviolet light. The chemistry is identical to transition eyeglass lenses.

![Photochromic UV ink changing from transparent indoors to colorful in sunlight on a white shirt.](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/uv-color-change-activation-1024x572.webp)

UV Color Change ink remains invisible indoors and activates only under direct sunlight (UV index 3+).

**Technical Specifications:**

- **Activation threshold:** UV index 3+ (direct sunlight or high-intensity UV lamps). Indoors under standard LED/incandescent lighting, the ink remains transparent or near-transparent.
- **Color behavior:** The printed color is bright but translucent. It lacks the vivid saturation of standard plastisol because the photochromic capsules are inherently semi-transparent.
- **Garment restriction:** Light-colored garments only such as white, cream, silver. On colored garments, the shirt color mixes unpredictably with the activated ink color. A red UV ink on a blue shirt may appear purple; the result is not controllable.
- **Color options:** Limited palette. Standard photochromic dyes are available in blue, red, yellow, and violet. Custom color matching is not reliable due to the translucent nature of the activated state.
- **Wash durability:** 20-30 cycles. The microcapsules degrade with repeated washing and UV exposure.

**When to use UV color change:** Outdoor event promotions, pool party merchandise, summer camp shirts, interactive novelty designs where the "reveal" effect creates engagement.

**When NOT to use UV color change:** Dark garments, designs requiring precise color matching, long-term workwear, indoor-only applications.

### Shimmer and Metallic Ink

Shimmer ink is plastisol loaded with metallic flake particles like aluminum or mica-based, that reflect light and produce a sparkling, metallic finish. Available in standard silver, gold, bronze, and black shimmer.

![shimmer-ink-tshirt](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2016/12/shimmer-ink-tshirt-300x225-1.webp)

**Technical Specifications:**

- **Flake size:** 50-200 microns. Larger flakes produce more visible sparkle but reduce detail resolution.
- **Detail limitations:** Fine detail and small text fill in with flake density and appear flat grey. Halftone dots and thin outlines lose definition because the flakes physically block fine mesh openings.
- **Color limitations:** Standard silver, gold, bronze, black. Custom color shimmer (pink, blue, green) requires custom flake formulation. NW Custom Apparel has tested custom shimmer formulations and found that non-standard colors consistently produce a greyed, muted result rather than a vivid metallic tone. We do not recommend custom color shimmer for orders requiring brand-accurate color.
- **Garment compatibility:** Works on all garment colors, including dark substrates. Metallic flake provides inherent opacity.
- **Wash durability:** 30-40 cycles. Flakes can detach from the plastisol binder over time, reducing shimmer intensity.

**When to use shimmer:** Championship and award shirts, celebration merchandise, premium branding where a metallic accent communicates quality or achievement.

**When NOT to use shimmer:** Fine detail, small text, designs requiring precise non-metallic color matching.

![Comparison showing shimmer ink working on bold shapes but failing on small, fine text.](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/shimmer-ink-detail-limitation-1024x558.webp)

Metallic flakes provide a premium sparkle but will 'fill in' and blur fine details or small fonts.

## Process Effects: Gradients and Belt Printing

### Gradient and Halftone Blends

Gradients in screen printing are achieved through halftone dots, varying dot size and density to create the illusion of continuous tone. Unlike digital printing (DTG), where gradients print smoothly, screen-printed gradients inherently show dot patterns.

**Technical Specifications:**

- **Dot gain:** Screen-printed halftones gain 15-25% compared to the digital file due to ink spread on fabric. A 20% dot in your file prints at approximately 35-40% on a T-shirt.
- **Band prevention:** Gradients created in Adobe Illustrator often show visible banding when converted to halftones. Creating gradients in Adobe Photoshop with noise dithering produces smoother halftone transitions.
- **Mesh count requirement:** 200-305 mesh for fine halftone dots. Lower mesh counts (110-156) cannot hold fine dot detail; the dots simply fill in and produce muddy tones.
- **Color separation:** Each gradient color requires its own screen and halftone angle. Standard angles: Cyan 15°, Magenta 75°, Yellow 90°, Black 45°. Angle selection prevents moiré patterns.

**When to use gradients:** Photographic designs, vintage distressed effects, designs where tonal variation adds depth.

**When NOT to use gradients:** Orders requiring precise color matching (dot gain shifts perceived color), designs with small text over gradient areas (legibility loss), budget-sensitive orders (additional screens add cost).

### Belt Printing (All-Over Printing)

Belt printing uses oversized screens (typically 40+ inches wide) that cover the entire front or back of a garment in a single print. The garment is fed through the press on a belt rather than loaded on a traditional pallet.

**Technical Specifications:**

- **Screen size:** 40"–60" wide vs. standard 20"–23" screens.
- **Registration tolerance:** ±2–3mm. This is significantly looser than standard screen printing (±0.5mm). Designs must accommodate registration variance.
- **Design requirements:** Limited color palette (1–3 colors recommended). Colors should never touch each other in the design; registration gaps are visible and unavoidable.
- **Garment compatibility:** Best on flat, stable substrates. Fleece and stretchy knits distort under the large screen and produce registration issues.
- **Production volume:** Economical at 144+ pieces. Setup costs for oversized screens make small runs prohibitively expensive.

**When to use belt printing:** All-over pattern designs, large-scale graphic T-shirts, retro-style full-chest prints, event merchandise where scale creates impact.

**When NOT to use belt printing:** Multi-color designs with tight registration, small orders, designs with fine detail, garments with stretch or pile.

### Cost Impact

Specialty inks add $1.50–$3.50 per print location compared to standard plastisol. The adder reflects:

- Additive material cost (phosphorescent pigment, metallic flake, expanding agent)
- Production complexity (multiple passes for high density, flash requirements, slower press speeds)
- Lower mesh counts requiring more ink consumption per print

High density is the most expensive due to multiple print passes and extended production time. Super glow and UV color change are mid-range. For volume pricing that offsets per-piece adders, see our **[bulk screen printed T-shirts](https://nwcustomapparel.net/bulk-screen-printed-tees-order/)** quantity tiers. Puff and shimmer are the most economical specialty options.

## Design File Requirements for Specialty Inks

Before submitting artwork for a specialty ink order, confirm your file meets these specifications:

- **Vector format:** Adobe Illustrator (.ai), EPS, or PDF with outlined fonts
- **Minimum line weights:** 3pt for puff, 1/8" for suede, 4pt for shimmer
- **Color separation:** Each specialty ink requires its own screen. A design with standard red + puff white + shimmer gold requires three screens.
- **Mockup approval:** We produce a physical sew-out or print sample on your selected garment before full production. Specialty effects are difficult to simulate digitally.

If your artwork needs adjustment, our **artwork preparation guide** covers vector conversion, font outlining, and color separation standards.

## Specialty Ink Durability and Care Instructions

| **Effect** | **Expected Wash Life** | **Care Recommendation** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Puff | 25–40 cycles | Wash inside-out, cold water, hang dry |
| High density | 30–50 cycles | Wash inside-out, avoid high-heat drying |
| Suede | 20–35 cycles | Gentle cycle, no fabric softener (coats nap) |
| Super glow | 15–25 cycles | Cold water only, hang dry, minimal detergent |
| UV color change | 20–30 cycles | Cold water, hang dry, limit UV exposure when not worn |
| Shimmer | 30–40 cycles | Wash inside-out, avoid bleach |

**Pacific Northwest climate note:** Washington's cool, humid climate extends air-drying times. If your crew relies on fast turnaround between washes, plan for longer dry times with hang-dry specialty prints or order additional garments to rotate.

## When to Skip Specialty Inks

Specialty effects are not always the right choice. Use standard plastisol when:

- Your design has fine detail or small text
- You need maximum wash durability (workwear subject to industrial laundering)
- Budget constraints make the per-piece adder prohibitive
- Your brand requires precise color matching (metallic and photochromic inks have limited palettes)
- Turnaround is critical (specialty inks require additional setup and sampling time)

For designs with photographic detail, unlimited colors, or small quantities where screen setup costs are uneconomical, **[DTG printing for designs that need softer feel](https://nwcustomapparel.net/direct-to-garment/)** is the better method.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can you print specialty inks on dark garments?**

Metallic inks (shimmer) and high density work on dark garments. Puff works on dark garments but loses some visual impact. Super glow and UV color change do not work on dark garments — the effects are invisible or unpredictable.

**How much do specialty inks cost compared to standard plastisol?**

Specialty inks add $1.50–$3.50 per print location. High density is at the top of that range due to multiple print passes. Puff and shimmer are at the lower end.

**Will puff ink work with my detailed logo?**

Only if your logo uses bold shapes with minimum 3pt line weights. Fine detail, small text, and sharp angles blur or disappear during puff expansion. We review your artwork and recommend standard plastisol or high density if puff is unsuitable.

**How long does glow-in-the-dark ink last?**

Super glow ink maintains usable brightness for 15–25 home wash cycles. After that, phosphorescent particle degradation reduces glow intensity. For long-term applications, standard plastisol with reflective tape is more durable.

**Can I mix multiple specialty effects on one shirt?**

Yes, within production constraints. A design can combine standard plastisol + puff + shimmer, with each effect requiring its own screen. Cost increases per screen, and registration between effects must accommodate each ink's physical behavior.

**Do you offer water-based or discharge specialty effects?**

Specialty additives are formulated for plastisol base. Water-based and discharge inks have different chemical structures that do not reliably accept puff, metallic, or phosphorescent additives. For soft-hand specialty effects, we recommend discharge printing for the base design and standard plastisol specialty accents.

**What is the minimum order for belt printing?**

Belt printing requires oversized screens and extended setup. The minimum is 72 pieces. For smaller orders requiring large-scale graphics, we recommend DTG or DTF as alternatives.
