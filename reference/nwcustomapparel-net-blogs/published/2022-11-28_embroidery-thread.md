# Best Embroidery Threads for Hand and Machine Use

Choosing the wrong embroidery thread can lead to a mess of broken strands, uneven stitches, and dull results. Whether you're hand-stitching a delicate piece or running a high-speed embroidery machine, thread choice makes a huge difference. But with the right guidance, you can select the perfect thread to enhance your work's beauty and durability. This article will take you through the best types of embroidery thread, their uses, and how to choose the perfect one for your project.

## What Is Embroidery Thread?

It's a special type of thread used specifically for decorative stitching in both hand and machine embroidery. It is designed to be stronger and more vibrant than regular sewing thread, ensuring durability and a smooth finish. Available in various materials such as **cotton, polyester, rayon, and silk**, embroidery thread offers different levels of sheen, texture, and strength. Each type of thread is suited for specific projects, from intricate hand-stitched designs to bold machine embroidery.

## Types of Embroidery Threads

### 1. Cotton Threads

Cotton is a classic, soft thread. You’ll find two main types: **stranded cotton** and **pearl cotton**. Stranded cotton, like DMC, is versatile, with multiple plies that you can separate for detailed designs. Pearl cotton, on the other hand, is a bit thicker and gives a nice texture, perfect for **cross-stitch** or **whitework**.

It's made of natural fiber cotton of high strength. It does not elongate when stretched but breaks instead. Dyes may be colorfast. The lubricity of this fiber is low, but it will cause lint to form from abrasion rather than stretch. This thread is considered the standard for fine monograms. The machine's hook area requires regular cleaning if you are using cotton bobbins.

### 2. Rayon Threads

Rayon thread is shiny, smooth,comes in several weights and grades. It works best in **machine embroidery** where its high sheen really stands out. However, it's a bit more delicate and can break easily compared to polyester. So Embroiderers must ensure their embroidery thread is designed for high-speed Tajima embroidery machines.

### 3. Polyester Threads

For durability and colorfastness, **polyester** is hard to beat. It resists wear and tear and holds up to washing, which is why it's a favorite for embroidering items like **towels** and **clothing**. **Isacord** and **Madeira** are top choices for machine use.

It's made from artificially created fibers derived from petroleum products, and comes in several grades and sizes. Generally stronger than rayon, polyester tends to elongate before bursting or breaking. This stretch can cause specific loop formation problems under improper tensioning. The lubricity and elongation of this thread also can cause it to loop when sewn with stitches placed too tightly together and/or sewn with too small a needle eye and too light a take-up spring snap.

#### Stretching of Polyester Thread

Also, after extreme stretching, many brands of polyester will contract back to their original size and cause a type of pucker around sewn areas. Polyester fiber melts before burns and suffers less from UV exposure than other threads. Colors are generally colorfast and can handle bleach. Still, some may react to dye strippers in commercial laundries if combined with certain industrial chemicals used at wearer work sites.

### 4. Silk Threads

If you're going for luxury, **silk thread** is the way to go. It’s smooth, strong, and gives a lovely finish. It’s best for intricate **hand embroidery**, but it can be pricey and a bit harder to find.

### 5. Metallic Threads

Need a bit of sparkle? **Metallic threads** are perfect for decorative touches. They’re tricky to work with, but brands like **Sulky** and **Madeira** have options that run smoothly on both hand and machine embroidery.

This thread is usually a rayon core wrapped with shiny Mylar strips to offer a metallic sheen. The dyed under the thread does not sparkle or glitter if the Mylar strips off the core. As a rayon core, it offers little elongation, and many brands break easily under higher tension. Because of the Mylar stiffness, thread breaks from uneven flow through the needle, and off-timing problems with the hook are common. This is one of the more expensive threads to produce and use.

### 6. Other Types

**Yarn** - Thread that is generally thicker than standard embroidery weights and may not have a consistent or tight twist. Composite yarns may include a weave of metallic Mylar into the fiber for a flecked appearance.

**Poly/Cotton Thread** – A blend of cotton and polyester, giving a thicker buildup, less elongation, and good strength. Straight cotton colors sheen compares to rayon and modern polyester. Embroidery colors are usually colorfast and bleachfast. It is sometimes used for bobbins.

**Acrylic Thread** - Acrylic is a type that is made from manmade acrylic fibers; this thread has a durability of wear over that offered by rayon but may not be as tough as polyester. It is offered in two weights for embroidery. Some colors may not be colorfast. It melts before burning and may suffer sun rot from UV exposure, mainly if treated with chemicals used to protect the vinyl.

**Embroidery Floss** - Embroidery floss is a [type of embroidery](https://nwcustomapparel.net/types-of-embroidery-techniques/) thread with a loosely woven yarn, usually measures in deniers and used for merrow machine edges on patches. Likewise, rayon or polyester offers good sheen and colorfastness.

## Hand Embroidery Threads

When it comes to hand embroidery, the thread you use can make or break your project. Cotton is a go-to for most hand-stitchers due to its flexibility. For example, **DMC stranded cotton** is ideal for delicate designs because it allows you to control the thickness by separating the strands. Silk and **variegated threads** add a unique flair but require more skill.

**Colorfastness** is important too, especially when your work might be washed. Always test threads for bleeding before committing to large projects.

## Machine Embroidery Threads

Machine embroidery needs tougher threads that can handle high-speed stitching without snapping. **Polyester** and **rayon** are the most popular for this. Polyester threads like **Isacord** provide durability, while rayon threads give a shinier finish, which is ideal for logos or decorative pieces.

Machine threads often come in larger **cones**, so make sure you match the thread weight and adjust the **tension** settings on your embroidery machine accordingly. This prevents thread breakage and ensures smooth stitching.

## Embroidery Thread Weights and Cones

Choosing the right thread **weight** is crucial for getting the look you want. A **40-weight thread** is the standard for machine embroidery, giving a balanced look that's not too thick or too thin. For finer details, you may want a **60-weight** thread, which is thinner and ideal for small lettering or intricate designs.

Thread **cones** come in various sizes, from small spools for hand embroidery to large cones for machine use. Be sure to stock up based on the scale of your project.

## How to Select the Right Thread for Your Project

Different threads work better on different **fabrics**. Cotton threads are ideal for lightweight fabrics like linen or muslin, while polyester threads excel on sturdier materials like denim or canvas. If you're embroidering something that needs to withstand frequent washing, like a pillowcase or shirt, polyester's durability will ensure it lasts.

Match the thread **finish** to your project’s goal. A **matte** cotton might be better for a vintage feel, while a shiny **rayon** or **metallic** thread can add sparkle to modern designs.

## Common Thread Problems and Solutions

Thread problems? You’re not alone. Breakage, tangling, and **fraying** are all too common. Using the wrong thread tension or needle size can increase these issues. Here are a few quick fixes:

- **Thread Breakage**: Adjust machine tension or use a stronger thread like polyester.
- **Tangling**: Avoid long strands when stitching by hand, and always pull gently.
- **Fraying**: Choose a smoother thread like rayon, especially for dense stitching.

### THREAD TERMINOLOGY

**Elongation** - A property measured by how far embroidery thread will stretch under tension when sewing before it breaks. Often memory will allow a thread to return to regular length if allowed to sit under pressure. This elasticity, if higher tensions, make elasticity not alluring for sewing.

**Lubricity** - A measure of how much lubricate the thread is to pass through the eye and sewing area. Thread generally passes 80 or more times through the needle eye, and the garment pulls by the take-up lever and spring-in action with the needle to make a stitch. So, how slick it is in the running through the eye is essential to surface wear on the thread and stitch formation if low lubricity causes elongation and loop problems.

Equally important, pass the thread through a silicone lubricator to increase the thread's lubricity. Furthermore, to transfer the lubricant to the thread and needle as it sews, try using a piece of backing with lubricate.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

**How do I choose between cotton and polyester?**

If you want softness and a natural look, go for cotton. For durability and resistance to fading, choose polyester.

**What type of embroidery thread is best for beginners?**

For beginners, cotton threads like **DMC stranded cotton** is a great option due to its versatility and ease of use. It's soft, easy to separate into strands, and widely available. Polyester threads are also beginner-friendly for machine embroidery because they are strong and resist breakage.

**How do I prevent embroidery thread from tangling?**

To avoid tangling, cut shorter lengths of thread, around 18 inches. Using a **thread conditioner** like beeswax or silicone can also help threads glide smoothly and reduce friction, which causes tangles.

**What’s the difference between embroidery thread and regular sewing thread?**

Embroidery thread is typically designed for decoration and has a higher sheen compared to regular sewing thread. It's also more flexible and stronger to handle the demands of decorative stitching. Regular sewing thread is usually made for seams and structural purposes.

**Can embroidery thread be used for machine sewing?**

While **embroidery thread** can be used in machines, it’s not recommended for general sewing. It’s designed for decorative work and lacks the strength for seams. For machine sewing, use regular polyester or cotton sewing thread.

**How do I store embroidery thread to maintain its quality?**

Store embroidery thread in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight to avoid fading. Use thread organizers, bobbin cases, or racks to prevent tangling. Keeping threads covered or in sealed containers helps protect them from dust and moisture.
