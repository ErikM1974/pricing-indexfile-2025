# The Pioneering Story of Washington's First Commercial Embroidery Machine

The story of Northwest Custom Apparel is a story of innovation, and it all began with a piece of equipment that, by today’s standards, would be considered a relic. Back in the late 1970s, the idea of a machine that could automatically stitch intricate designs onto fabric was a futuristic concept, especially in the Pacific Northwest. Yet, it was in this era that a single machine arrived in Washington state, setting the stage for what would become a cornerstone of the local custom apparel industry.

This is the story of that first machine and how its arrival at Northwest Custom Apparel, then a small upstart, marked the dawn of commercial machine embroidery in the Seattle and Tacoma area. It’s a journey from groundbreaking, slow-moving technology to today’s high-speed, high-tech marvels, all while staying rooted in a legacy of quality and pioneering spirit.

## A Pioneering Purchase: The First Commercial Machine in Tacoma, Washington

![first embroidery machine in washington](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/first-lmachine-300x182-1.webp)

The year was 1978. At the time, Jim Mickelson, the founder of Northwest Custom Apparel, was already a savvy entrepreneur. After successfully selling a small run of embroidered caps to his fellow employees on the Alaska Pipeline, he saw an opportunity that no one else in the region had. To meet the growing demand, he took a risk and made a monumental purchase: the very first commercial production embroidery machine in Washington state.

The machine was a Tajima, a brand that was at the cutting edge of technology at the time. It was a massive, mechanical device—a far cry from today's sleek, computerized models. Jim had the machine shipped to Tacoma and, with no one to teach him, he taught himself the intricacies of its operation. This pioneering purchase wasn’t just a business investment; it was the birth of a new industry in the Pacific Northwest.

## Then vs. Now: The Incredible Evolution of Embroidery Technology

Looking back at that first machine today, it’s a powerful reminder of how far technology has come. The original equipment, which felt like a futuristic wonder at the time, is a stark contrast to the machines we use in our modern facilities today.

- **Speed:** That first Tajima had a top running speed of a fixed **220 stitches per minute**. This was considered lightning-fast for its era. By comparison, our current state-of-the-art machines operate at speeds as high as **1,200 stitches per minute**—a more than five-fold increase in efficiency. This dramatic leap in speed means we can produce orders faster and handle higher volumes without compromising on quality.
- **Color Capacity:** The old mechanical machines were limited to a mere **6 colors** at a time. Every color change was a manual, time-consuming process. Today, our advanced machines can handle up to **15 colors** in a single design, allowing for incredibly rich, detailed, and vibrant creations without any manual intervention. This gives our clients far more creative freedom and design flexibility.
- **Digitization:** The initial process for creating a design was laborious and manual. Designs had to be physically "punched" into paper rolls that the machine would read—an art form in itself. Today, thanks to the power of computers, [designs are digitized](https://nwcustomapparel.net/logo-digitizing-for-embroidery/) with specialized software, then sent directly to our machines via a network. This ensures pinpoint accuracy, perfect replication, and a streamlined workflow that was unimaginable decades ago.

The clunky, beautiful “boat anchors” of the past laid the groundwork for the powerful, precise, and fast machinery that fills our factory today.
