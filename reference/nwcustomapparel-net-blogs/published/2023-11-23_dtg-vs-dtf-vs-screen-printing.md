# DTG vs DTF vs Screen Printing: Which Method Is Right for Your Order?

No single printing method is best. Each one is correct for a different combination of fabric, order size, and design type. Here is the short answer before the full explanation:

- **DTG:** Right for full-color, photographic, or complex designs on 100% cotton garments in quantities from 1 to approximately 50 pieces.
- **DTF:** Right for polyester, synthetic blends, and any garment where DTG pretreatment adhesion is unreliable. Also works on cotton. No minimum.
- **Screen printing:** Right for bulk orders of 24+ pieces with simple designs of 1 to 6 colors where per-unit cost at scale matters most.

### Why Integrated Pretreatment Matters for DTG Quality

![Kornit DTG printing System](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/kornit-dtg-machines-for-industrial-production.webp)

Pretreatment is the step most buyers never see but most directly affects whether their DTG print survives washing. **Dark garments require a chemical pretreatment solution applied to the fabric before printing to create adhesion for the white ink underbase.** When pretreatment is applied manually (sprayed by hand or with a separate machine) inconsistent coverage causes uneven adhesion. Undercoated areas wash out. Overcoated areas stain or stiffen.

The Kornit Storm Hexa printer integrates pretreatment directly into the print cycle using a wet-on-wet process, Kornit's patented method of applying pretreatment and starting ink deposition in the same pass without a separate cure step between them. This eliminates the manual pretreat error that is the most common cause of DTG wash durability failures at shops using separate pretreat equipment.

### DTG Fabric Compatibility - Why 100% Cotton Produces the Best Results

Water-based pigment inks penetrate and bond with natural cotton fibers. On 100% cotton, the ink becomes part of the fabric structure. On polyester, the ink sits on a non-absorbent synthetic surface and adhesion depends entirely on the pretreatment chemistry. **For orders on 80%+ cotton blends, DTG performs well. Below 80% cotton, DTF is the more reliable choice.**

*Natural fibers such as 100% cotton provide the best results for DTG because they absorb water-based inks effectively. Synthetic fabrics like polyester are more challenging due to dye migration and ink adhesion issues.* By [ScreenPrinting.com, DTG vs DTF comparison analysis](https://www.screenprinting.com/blogs/news/dtg-vs-dtf-printing-a-comprehensive-comparison)

## What Is DTF Printing and When Should You Use It?

**DTF is the right choice when your fabric is polyester or synthetic blend, when you need small quantities with no minimum, or when your design requires full color on a dark substrate across fabric types that DTG cannot handle reliably.**

DTF printing is the fastest-growing decoration method in the custom apparel industry because it solves the two problems that limit both DTG and screen printing: fabric restriction and minimum order quantity.

### How DTF Printing Works

![How DTF Printing Works](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/steve-n-joe-doing-DTF-process-scaled.webp)

DTF printing has two stages separated by a transfer step. First, the design is printed onto a PET (polyester) transfer film using pigment inks in CMYK plus White configuration. Immediately after printing, hot-melt adhesive powder is applied to the wet ink surface and cured with heat to bond the powder to the ink layer. The result is a finished transfer film that can be applied to any fabric.

In the second stage, the transfer film is placed face-down on the garment and heat-pressed at 300-325°F for 10-15 seconds. The heat melts the adhesive, bonding the design to the fabric surface. The film is then peeled away, leaving the design adhered to the garment.

See the [full DTF printing process explained including press parameters and fabric behavior](https://nwcustomapparel.net/dtf-printing-how-it-works/).

**Key difference from DTG:** DTF does not print directly onto the garment. It prints onto film and transfers. This is why it works on any fabric — the adhesive bonds to the surface regardless of whether the fabric is cotton, polyester, nylon, leather, or a technical blend.

### DTF Fabric Compatibility - The Widest Range of Any Method

DTF prints on cotton, polyester, nylon, spandex blends, softshell, performance knits, synthetic workwear, leather, and any fabric that tolerates heat press application at 300-325°F. No pretreatment is required. Fabric color does not affect the process because white, black, and neon fabrics all accept DTF transfers the same way.

**For polyester workwear, performance athletic wear, and moisture-wicking shirts, DTF is the correct method.** Screen printing on polyester above 50% content requires low-bleed plastisol at a reduced cure temperature (275-280°F) to prevent dye migration. DTF avoids this problem entirely because the adhesive layer bonds at 300-325°F without touching the polyester dyes.

### DTF Wash Durability and Hand Feel

DTF prints are cured correctly at 300-325°F for 10-15 seconds to resist cracking, peeling, and fading for 50+ wash cycles under normal laundering. The adhesive layer is flexible, not brittle, it stretches with the fabric rather than cracking at bend points. **On large solid fill areas, DTF has a slightly heavier surface feel than DTG on cotton.** On logo-sized prints (left chest, 4 inches or smaller), most buyers do not notice the surface layer.

Care instructions for DTF prints: machine wash cold or warm, tumble dry low. Avoid ironing directly on the printed area — iron inside-out or place a pressing cloth over the design if ironing is necessary.

### When DTF Is Not the Right Choice

- **Hi-Vis ANSI safety apparel:** heat press application may damage retroreflective tape and void ANSI certification, screen printing is required
- **Maximum softness on 100% cotton:** DTG on cotton produces a softer hand feel because ink penetrates fibers rather than sitting on the surface
- **High volume with simple designs:** screen printing is more cost-effective above 100 pieces on 1-4 color designs

## What Is Screen Printing and When Should You Use It?

**Screen printing is the right choice when your order exceeds 24 pieces, your design has 6 or fewer solid colors, and per-unit cost at scale is the priority.**

Screen printing is the oldest commercial fabric decoration method and still the most cost-effective for bulk orders. As quantity increases, the fixed setup cost (per color screen) amortizes across more pieces and per-unit cost drops significantly. No other method produces lower per-unit cost above 100 pieces on simple designs.

### How NW Custom Apparel Produces Screen Printing

![screen printer machine](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2016/12/T_Shirt_Screen_Printing_Machine_1-300x190-1.webp)

NW Custom Apparel runs in-house screen printing at our Milton, WA facility. Plastisol ink is the standard. Water-based and discharge inks are available. Each color in the design requires a separate screen burned from a film positive. Dark garments require a white plastisol underbase printed and flash-cured before color layers are applied. The finished print is cured in a conveyor dryer at 320°F.

In-house production means one point of contact from art approval to boxed shipment. No outsourcing to a third-party printer creates the quality gap that most screen printing complaints trace back to.

### Screen Printing and Polyester - The Dye Migration Problem

Standard plastisol ink on polyester fabric causes dye migration. **Polyester dyes turn to gas above 300°F in the conveyor dryer and migrate upward through the ink layer, shifting print colors permanently.** A white print on a red polyester shirt turns pink. A yellow print on royal blue polyester turns green. The shift cannot be reversed after curing.

The solution is low-bleed plastisol formulation, which cures at 275-280°F — below the dye volatilization threshold. NW Custom Apparel uses low-bleed plastisol automatically on all polyester and high-polyester-content orders. For orders on 100% polyester performance fabric, DTF is often the more reliable choice because it eliminates the dye migration variable entirely.

### Screen Printing Wash Durability

Plastisol screen prints on 100% cotton cured correctly at 320°F through the full ink deposit thickness last 25-50+ commercial wash cycles. The distinction between 25 and 50+ wash cycles depends primarily on cure temperature accuracy. Under-cured ink, where the surface reaches 320°F but the ink deposit core does not, cracks and washes out early. At full cure through the complete deposit, plastisol is the most durable method under commercial laundering among all three methods.

### The Only Method for Hi-Vis Safety Apparel

Screen printing is the **only ANSI-certified decoration method for Hi-Vis safety apparel.** ANSI/ISEA 107-2015 Class 2 and Class 3 garments must maintain specific minimum retroreflective tape coverage, embroidery hooping damages retroreflective tape, DTF heat press can compromise tape adhesion, and DTG is not suitable for Hi-Vis neon substrates. Screen printing [applies ink](https://nwcustomapparel.net/specialty-screen-printing-inks-effects/) to the background fabric areas only, leaving retroreflective tape untouched and ANSI certification intact.

For more best uses, see the [pros of screen printing guide](https://nwcustomapparel.net/advantages-disadvantages-screen-printing/).

### When Screen Printing Is Not the Right Choice

- **Orders under 24 pieces:** per-screen setup cost makes per-unit price uneconomical, so DTG or DTF is more cost-effective
- **Designs with 7 or more colors:** consider four-color CMYK process or DTG for complex multi-color artwork
- **Personalized pieces:** screen printing prints all pieces identically, DTG or DTF required for individual name or number customization
- **100% polyester performance fabric:** DTF is more reliable; low-bleed plastisol is available but adds production complexity

## Wash Durability Compared - The Number and the Methodology Behind It

Every comparison article quotes wash durability numbers. Very few explain what those numbers actually mean in practice. Here is the methodology behind the numbers used on this page.

|  |  |  |  |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Method** | **Claim** | **Laundering Condition** | **Primary Failure Mode** |
| DTG (Kornit Storm Hexa) | 40-50 washes | Cold water, gentle cycle, air dry or tumble dry low | Color fade on dark garments; edge cracking if under-cured or manual pretreat inconsistency |
| DTF | 50+ washes | Cold or warm water, tumble dry low - avoid high heat drying | Edge lifting on large solid fills if heat press temperature or dwell time was inconsistent during application |
| Screen printing (plastisol) | 25-50+ washes | Commercial laundering - hot water, industrial dryer, weekly frequency | Cracking if under-cured (surface hit 320F but deposit core did not); color fade on light plastisol deposits |

The key distinction is laundering condition. Construction crews washing Hi-Vis shirts in commercial machines weekly degrade any print faster than tech company employees washing polo shirts on a home gentle cycle monthly. When you compare 40-50 washes (DTG, cold gentle) against 25-50+ washes (screen printing, commercial hot wash), screen printing under identical commercial laundering conditions often outlasts DTG on the same garment.

**What this means practically:** For construction and trades uniforms washed multiple times weekly in commercial machines, plastisol screen printing on cotton is the most durable choice. For corporate and event apparel washed occasionally in consumer machines, DTG and DTF both perform within their rated wash cycle ranges.

## Frequently Asked Questions - DTG vs DTF vs Screen Printing

**Which printing method lasts the longest?**

Screen printing with plastisol ink on 100% cotton under commercial laundering lasts the longest — 25-50+ wash cycles in hot water industrial machines. DTF follows at 50+ wash cycles under normal consumer laundering. DTG on Kornit Storm Hexa performs at 40-50 washes under cold-water gentle-cycle conditions. The comparison changes depending on laundering intensity, screen printing on cotton under commercial laundering outlasts DTG on cotton under the same conditions.

**Is DTF better than DTG in 2026?**

Neither method is universally better. DTF is better on polyester, synthetic fabrics, and mixed-fabric orders where DTG adhesion is unreliable. DTG on the Kornit Storm Hexa is better on 100% cotton where maximum softness and ink integration into fibers matters, and for personalized orders where each piece needs a different design. The correct question is which method is better for your specific fabric and quantity.

**Does NW Custom Apparel use Kornit Storm Hexa printers for DTG?**

Yes. NW Custom Apparel's DTG shop runs the Kornit Storm Hexa — a 16-head industrial direct-to-garment system with 7-color ink channels (CMYK plus Red, Green, and White), 1,200 DPI resolution, integrated inline pretreatment, and up to 170 garments per hour production speed. The Red and Green channels allow accurate reproduction of brand colors that standard CMYK DTG machines cannot match.

**What printing method is cheapest for small orders?**

For 1-23 pieces, DTG and DTF have no setup fee and no minimum, they are the only cost-effective options below 24 pieces. DTG is cheaper on 100% cotton with complex designs. DTF is the better choice on polyester or synthetic fabric. Screen printing below 24 pieces is rarely cost-effective because the per-screen setup fee dominates the per-unit cost at low quantities. See [no minimum DTG printing for small Washington state orders](https://nwcustomapparel.net/no-minimum-dtg-printing/).

**What printing method is cheapest for large orders?**

Screen printing is the most cost-effective method above 100 pieces with designs of 6 or fewer colors. The per-screen setup fee amortizes across the full quantity, a 6-color setup on a 500-piece run costs a small fraction of the same setup on a 24-piece run. DTG and DTF have no volume discount mechanism; per-piece cost is fixed regardless of quantity.

**Can you use DTG on polyester?**

Standard DTG, including the Kornit Storm Hexa, performs on polyester but with lower reliability than on cotton. The machine is rated for polyester fabric, but water-based pigment inks adhere better to natural cotton fibers than synthetic ones. For orders on 100% polyester or high-polyester-content fabric, DTF is the more reliable choice with consistent adhesion across all synthetic substrates without the dye migration variable.

**What is the difference between DTF and heat transfer vinyl (HTV)?**

Both DTF and HTV use a heat press to apply a design to fabric, but they are different processes. HTV is a pre-cut colored vinyl film - designs are cut on a plotter, weeded, and heat-pressed. It is best for simple, single-color designs. DTF prints full-color photographic designs onto PET film with adhesive powder, producing photorealistic results with no color count limitation. DTF is a full digital print process; HTV is a vinyl cutting process.

**Which method works best on dark shirts?**

All three methods handle dark shirts but through different mechanisms. DTG uses a white ink underbase printed first by the Kornit Storm Hexa — the integrated pretreatment and wet-on-wet process ensures consistent underbase adhesion. DTF prints white ink on the transfer film automatically, dark fabric color is irrelevant. Screen printing prints a white plastisol underbase first and flash-cures it before color layers. All three produce accurate colors on dark garments. DTF is the most consistent across different dark fabric types because the fabric color never contacts the ink directly.
