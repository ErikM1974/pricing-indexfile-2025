# Custom Embroidered Aprons for Commercial Kitchens: What We Produce for Restaurants, Breweries, and Culinary Teams

Restaurants destroy aprons. Hot grease, sharp knives, industrial washing machines, and twelve-hour shifts turn standard kitchen wear into rags within a month. ## Apron Styles We Embroider: Bib, Waist, and Bistro

Commercial kitchens use three apron styles. Each has specific embroidery requirements.

1. **Bib aprons** cover the chest and lap. They have adjustable neck straps and waist ties. The embroidery area is the chest panel, typically 4 to 6 inches wide. We center the logo or name between the straps. Bib aprons work for chefs, line cooks, and prep staff who need full coverage.
2. **Waist aprons** cover the lap only. They tie at the waist and leave the chest exposed. The embroidery area is the front panel or a corner pocket, typically 2.5 to 3.5 inches wide. Waist aprons work for servers, bartenders, and front-of-house staff who need mobility and a clean look.
3. **Bistro aprons** are longer waist aprons that extend below the knee. They have a more formal appearance. The embroidery area is the upper front panel. Bistro aprons work for upscale restaurants, catering companies, and culinary demonstration teams.

We do not recommend embroidery on the straps themselves. The constant adjustment and tension cause thread breaks and distortion. We place logos on stable panels that do not stretch during wear.

## Fabrics That Survive Commercial Kitchens

The fabric determines how long the apron lasts and how the embroidery holds. We work with 4 primary materials.

**Canvas** is the standard for commercial kitchens. It is heavyweight, abrasion-resistant, and accepts embroidery cleanly. A 10-ounce cotton canvas apron survives industrial washing and maintains its shape. The embroidery sinks into the weave and stays flat. We produce canvas aprons for breweries, barbecue restaurants, and any kitchen where grease and heat are constant.

**Denim** is softer than canvas but still durable. It works for casual restaurants, coffee shops, and bakeries where the aesthetic is rustic or artisanal. Denim frays at the edges over time. We recommend merrowed or folded edges on denim aprons to prevent unraveling. The embroidery on denim requires a denser underlay because the fabric is thinner than canvas.

**Polyester blends** resist stains and dry quickly. They work for catering teams and outdoor food service where aprons get wet. Polyester does not accept standard embroidery thread as cleanly as cotton. We use polyester thread on polyester aprons to match the fiber content and prevent puckering.

**Waxed canvas** is water-resistant and stiff. It works for oyster bars, seafood markets, and outdoor cooking demonstrations. The wax coating makes embroidery challenging. We use a water-soluble topping and reduced stitch speed to penetrate the coating without gumming the needles. Not every embroidery shop will attempt this. We do.

## How We Embroider Aprons: The Production Process

Apron embroidery is not the same as polo shirt embroidery. The fabric is thicker. The seams are heavier. The pockets and straps create obstacles. We have developed a specific process for aprons over forty-eight years.

**Step 1: Hooping.** We use clamp hoops instead of standard round hoops for aprons. Clamp hoops hold the fabric flat without stretching the straps or warping the panel. A warped panel produces a distorted logo.

**Step 2: Stabilizer selection.** Canvas and denim require cut-away stabilizer for dense embroidery. The stabilizer stays in the apron after production and prevents the logo from sagging. Waist aprons with lighter fabric may use tear-away stabilizer if the stitch count is low.

**Step 3: Needle and thread.** We use 75/11 sharp needles for canvas and denim. Ballpoint needles snag on heavy weave. We use Madeira or Isacord polyester thread for commercial aprons because it survives bleach and industrial detergent better than rayon thread.

**Step 4: Stitch speed.** We run at 800 RPM for apron embroidery, not our standard 1,100 RPM. The slower speed reduces needle deflection on thick fabric and produces cleaner edges.

**Step 5: Trim and inspection.** We trim jump stitches by hand. Machine trimming leaves thread tails that unravel in washing. We inspect every apron under LED light for thread breaks, puckering, and placement accuracy.

Our standard placement tolerance is plus or minus 2 millimeters. For aprons, we tighten this to plus or minus 1 millimeter because the logo is more visible and the fabric is less forgiving.

## What to Embroider: Logo, Name, or Both

Commercial kitchens need clear identification. Customers should know who is cooking. Managers should know who is working. The embroidery must serve both purposes.

**Restaurant logos** on the chest panel establish brand identity. We size them between 3 and 4 inches wide for bib aprons, 2.5 to 3 inches for waist aprons. The logo must be simple enough to reproduce in thread. Fine details and small text do not translate well to embroidery on thick fabric.

**Staff names** below the logo identify the individual. We use block fonts at 8 millimeter height minimum. Script fonts look elegant but become illegible after repeated washing. Names are essential for restaurants that want customers to address staff directly.

**Titles or roles** such as "Chef," "Sous Chef," or "Manager" help customers identify authority. We place these above or beside the logo depending on the apron style.

**What does not work:** Photographs, gradients, and complex illustrations. These require DTG or DTF printing, not embroidery. If your design includes a photographic element, we recommend embroidery for the text and logo, and a separate printed patch for the image.

## When to Choose Embroidery vs. Screen Printing for Aprons

| Factor | Embroidery | Screen Printing |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Fabric** | Canvas, denim, heavy cotton | Lightweight cotton, polyester blends |
| **Durability** | Survives 200+ industrial washes | Survives 50–100 washes before fading |
| **Appearance** | Raised, textured, professional | Flat, vivid, casual |
| **Cost at volume** | Higher per unit, no setup for reorders | Lower per unit, setup fee per color |
| **Minimum order** | 6 pieces | 24 pieces |
| **Best for** | Upscale restaurants, breweries, culinary schools | Casual cafes, event staff, promotional aprons |

**Our recommendation:** Choose embroidery for aprons that represent your brand daily and need to survive commercial washing. Choose screen printing for event aprons, one-time promotions, or casual staff where cost matters more than longevity.

NW Custom Apparel produces both methods in-house. We do not outsource and recommend the method that fits your actual use case, not the method that generates the highest invoice.

## Turnaround and Pricing for Custom Apron Orders

**Standard turnaround:** 7 to 10 business days for embroidered aprons. This includes file digitizing for new logos, sample approval if requested, and production.

**Rush turnaround:** 3 to 5 business days for orders under 50 pieces with no sample approval. Rush fees apply.

**Pricing factors:**

- **Stitch count:** Higher stitch counts require more production time and thread. A simple name costs less than a detailed logo.
- **Quantity:** Embroidery has a high setup cost per design but low per-unit cost at volume. The break-even point is typically around 24 pieces.
- **Fabric:** Canvas and denim cost more than lightweight cotton. Waxed canvas costs the most due to slower production speed.
- **Digitizing:** One-time fee of $100 for new logos depending on complexity. Reorders use the same file at no additional charge.

**Sample policy:** For orders over 100 pieces, we produce a physical sample on the actual apron for your approval. For smaller orders, we send a digital proof. Production begins only after approval.

## FAQ

**What is the minimum order for custom embroidered aprons?**

Six pieces. This is our standard embroidery minimum. For smaller orders, we recommend DTG or DTF printing on lightweight aprons with no minimum.

**How long does custom apron embroidery take?**

Standard turnaround is 7 to 10 business days. Rush orders take 3 to 5 business days with priority scheduling. Sample approval adds 2 to 3 days.

**Can you embroider on aprons I already own?**

Yes. We offer [contract embroidery on customer-supplied items](https://nwcustomapparel.net/services/contract-embroidery-customer-supplied-items/). You ship the aprons to our Milton facility. We embroider and return them. Minimums and pricing are the same as for aprons we source.

**What file format do I need for apron embroidery?**

Vector files: AI, EPS, or PDF with editable layers. If you only have a JPEG or PNG, our art department can recreate it as a vector file for a fee.

**Will the embroidery survive industrial washing?**

Yes. We use polyester thread and cut-away stabilizer on commercial-grade aprons. The embroidery survives 200+ industrial wash cycles. Rayon thread fades faster and is not recommended for commercial kitchens.

**Can you add individual names to each apron?**

Yes. We use variable data embroidery to produce individual names without stopping production between aprons. This is standard for uniform programs and culinary schools.
