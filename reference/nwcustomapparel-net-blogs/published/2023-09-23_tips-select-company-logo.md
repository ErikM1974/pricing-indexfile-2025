# How to Choose a Company Logo That Works on Custom Apparel

## Your Logo Type Determines Your Decoration Options

A logo that looks perfect on a website header may fail on a left-chest polo. A detailed illustration that wins design awards may cost $500 to digitize for embroidery and still sew like a blob. The logo type you choose at the start of your business dictates every decoration decision you will make for the next decade.

At Northwest Custom Apparel in Milton, Washington, we have embroidered and printed thousands of company logos since 1977. We have seen startups choose beautiful, complex logos that become production nightmares. We have seen established companies rebrand to simpler marks specifically because their old logo could not reproduce cleanly on workwear.

This guide covers how to choose a logo type with apparel decoration in mind. Not general branding advice. Production reality from our floor.

## The Six Logo Types and How They Perform on Apparel

### Wordmark (Logotype)

A wordmark uses your company name in a stylized font. Google. Coca-Cola. Disney.

**Apparel performance:** Wordmarks are the safest choice for apparel. They

- Embroider cleanly because text is predictable for digitizing software
- Screen print well because each letter is a solid shape, and
- Scale to any size without losing clarity.

**Our recommendation:** If your business name is distinctive and not generic (not "Seattle Plumbing" or "Tacoma Auto"), a wordmark is your lowest-risk option for decoration. Choose a font with clean lines and consistent stroke weight. Avoid script fonts with thin connecting lines that disappear at small sizes.

**Decoration methods:** Embroidery, screen printing, DTG all handle wordmarks without issue.

### Lettermark

A lettermark uses initials instead of the full name. IBM. HBO. NASA.

**Apparel performance:** Lettermarks work well on apparel if the letters are bold and spaced adequately. Two or three letters fit comfortably on a left-chest polo or a cap. More than four letters become cramped and hard to read at small sizes.

**Our recommendation:** Lettermarks are ideal for businesses with long names that would not fit on a shirt. "Northwest Custom Apparel" becomes "NWCA." But the initials must be meaningful. Random letters that do not connect to your name confuse customers and weaken brand recognition.

**Decoration methods:** Embroidery and screen printing are both suitable. DTG is unnecessary unless the lettermark includes gradients or photographic elements.

### Pictorial Mark

A pictorial mark is a recognizable image that represents your brand. Apple. Twitter. Shell.

**Apparel performance:** Pictorial marks vary widely in apparel feasibility. A simple apple silhouette embroiders cleanly. A detailed bird with feather texture does not. The simpler the image, the better it performs on fabric.

**Our recommendation:** If you choose a pictorial mark, test it at 3.5 inches wide (standard left-chest size) before finalizing. If details merge or disappear at that size, simplify the image or add a wordmark alongside it. A standalone pictorial mark without text requires massive brand recognition to be effective. Apple can do it. A new construction company in Tacoma cannot.

**Decoration methods:** Simple pictorial marks work with embroidery and screen printing. Complex images require DTG or significant simplification.

### Abstract Mark

An abstract mark is a unique geometric form that does not represent a recognizable object. Nike swoosh. Pepsi globe. Adidas stripes.

**Apparel performance:** Abstract marks are excellent for apparel if they are simple. The Nike swoosh is a single curved line. It embroiders in seconds, screens in one pass, and is readable at any size.

**Our recommendation:** Abstract marks are powerful but risky. A bad abstract mark looks like a random squiggle. A good one becomes iconic. If you choose this route, invest in professional design and test the mark at embroidery size before committing. An abstract mark that requires 15,000 stitches to embroider adds cost to every garment for the life of your business.

**Decoration methods:** Simple abstract marks work with all methods. Complex abstract marks with gradients or fine lines require DTG.

### Mascot Logo

A mascot logo features a character that personifies the brand. KFC Colonel. Michelin Man. MailChimp monkey.

**Apparel performance:** Mascot logos are the most challenging for apparel. Characters have facial features, clothing, and proportions that do not shrink well. A mascot designed for a billboard becomes unrecognizable on a cap.

**Our recommendation:** If your brand relies on a mascot (schools, sports teams, food brands), create a simplified version specifically for apparel. Remove facial detail. Reduce to two or three colors. Create a silhouette alternative for small applications. We have produced mascot embroidery for Tacoma schools. The versions that work are dramatically simpler than the full-color original.

**Decoration methods:** Full-color mascots require DTG or complex multi-color screen printing. Simplified mascots can screen print or embroider.

### Emblem Logo

An emblem logo combines text inside a symbol or badge. Harley Davidson shield. Starbucks siren in a circle. NFL shield.

**Apparel performance:** Emblems work well on apparel because the border contains the design and creates a defined shape. They look professional on polos, jackets, and caps. The risk is complexity. An emblem with internal text, decorative borders, and multiple colors becomes expensive to produce.

**Our recommendation:** Emblems are ideal for construction companies, security firms, and organizations that want a traditional, authoritative look. Limit internal text to a name and year. Avoid decorative flourishes that add stitches or screens without adding recognition. We have embroidered hundreds of emblem logos. The ones that age well are the simplest.

**Decoration methods:** Simple emblems work with embroidery and screen printing. Complex emblems with internal gradients require DTG.

## How Logo Type Affects Your Printing Budget

Your logo type directly impacts production cost across every order you place for the life of your business.

| Logo Type | Embroidery Cost | Screen Print Cost | DTG Cost | Long-Term Recommendation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Wordmark | Low (predictable text) | Low (1–2 colors) | Low | Best value over time |
| Lettermark | Low (2–4 letters) | Low (1–2 colors) | Low | Good for long names |
| Pictorial mark | Variable (depends on detail) | Variable (depends on colors) | Medium | Simplify for apparel |
| Abstract mark | Low to medium | Low to medium | Low | Excellent if simple |
| Mascot | High (detail = stitches) | High (detail = screens) | Medium | Create simplified version |
| Emblem | Medium to high | Medium to high | Medium | Limit internal complexity |

A wordmark that costs $8 to embroider on a polo will cost $8 on every polo for the next ten years. A complex mascot that costs $45 to embroider multiplies across every employee, every replacement, every new hire. The logo type you choose is not a one-time design decision. It is a recurring production cost.

## Color Count and Printing Method

The number of colors in your logo determines which decoration methods are practical.

### One-Color Logos

One-color logos are the most versatile. They work with single-color screen printing, single-thread embroidery, and heat transfer. They reproduce on any garment color by inverting (white on dark, black on light). Also, they age well because they do not rely on color trends.

**Our recommendation:** If you are starting a business and plan to use custom apparel, design a one-color version of your logo even if your primary brand uses multiple colors. The one-color version becomes your workhorse for uniforms, promotional items, and cost-sensitive orders.

### Two-Color Logos

Two-color logos add flexibility without major production complications. Screen printing requires two screens. Embroidery requires two thread changes. Both are standard operations.

**Our recommendation:** Choose colors with strong contrast. Navy and white work on light and dark garments. Red and yellow work on light garments but disappear on red shirts. Test your two-color logo on black, white, and your primary brand color before finalizing.

### Three or More Colors

Logos with three or more colors look vibrant on screens and business cards. On apparel, each additional color adds cost and complexity. Screen printing requires a separate screen per color. Embroidery requires additional thread changes and longer production time.

**Our recommendation:** If your brand requires three or more colors, plan for DTG printing on small orders and simplified two-color versions for bulk screen print and embroidery. We have produced four-color embroidered logos for clients who accepted the cost. Most businesses regret the decision when they reorder 100 pieces annually.

## When to Simplify Your Existing Logo

Some businesses come to us with a logo that already exists. It looks great on their website. It fails on apparel. We have three options.

**Option 1: Simplify for apparel.** Remove gradients. Reduce to two colors. Increase line weight. This creates a secondary logo mark that lives alongside your primary brand.

**Option 2: Redesign entirely.** If your logo is less than three years old and not widely recognized, a redesign may cost less than years of expensive decoration.

**Option 3: Accept higher production costs.** Some brands (luxury hotels, high-end restaurants) prioritize visual complexity over production efficiency. They budget for DTG printing on small orders and accept that bulk embroidery is not feasible.

We review existing logos for apparel feasibility at no charge. Send us your file. We will tell you which decoration methods work, which do not, and what simplification would cost if you choose that route.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Should I choose a pictorial mark or a wordmark for my construction company?**

A wordmark or emblem is usually better for construction and trade businesses. Pictorial marks require brand recognition that takes years to build. A wordmark with bold sans-serif lettering and a simple geometric element (a roofline, a tool silhouette) communicates your business immediately and embroiders cleanly on workwear.

**Can I use my full-color logo on all my apparel?**

You can, but it will cost more. Full-color logos require DTG printing for small orders or complex multi-screen setup for bulk orders. We recommend creating a one-color or two-color version for daily uniforms and reserving the full-color version for promotional items and special events.

**What if my designer already created a complex logo?**

You can then decide whether to simplify the logo or budget for higher decoration costs.

**How do I know if my logo will work on a cap?**

Caps have a curved surface and limited print area. Logos wider than 4 inches or taller than 2.25 inches will not fit. Complex shapes with internal detail lose clarity on curved panels. Send us your file. We will create a mockup showing exactly how your logo fits on a cap, a left-chest polo, and a full-back shirt.
