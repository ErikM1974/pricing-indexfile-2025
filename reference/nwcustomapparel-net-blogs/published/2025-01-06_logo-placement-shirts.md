## What Are the Standard Logo Placement Zones on a Shirt?

Every shirt has named placement zones. Each zone has an industry-accepted measurement anchor, a maximum print area, and use-case norms. Decorators use these terms in production. Buyers who know them write better spec sheets and receive fewer surprises.

![A map of standard apparel logo placement zones for front and back of a shirt.](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/universal-shirt-placement-zones-1024x558.webp)

Standard logo placement zones used by professional decorators to ensure brand consistency.

Knowing the zone names and their definitions lets you brief any decorator without ambiguity.

| Zone | Definition |
| --- | --- |
| Left chest | The area on the wearer's left side of the chest, approximately 3–4 inches below the collar seam. This is the industry standard for corporate uniforms and work shirts. |
| Right chest | The mirror position on the wearer's right side. Used for name tags, secondary logos, or flag patches. |
| Full front | The entire chest area from side seam to side seam, typically 3–4 inches below the collar and up to 12–16 inches wide. Used for event shirts, team jerseys, and bold brand statements. |
| Center chest | A subset of full front, centered horizontally, 2–3.5 inches below the collar, 6–12 inches wide. Common for retail-style chest prints. |
| Full back | The entire back panel from shoulder seam to shoulder seam, used for large team names, sponsor logos, or event graphics. |
| Back yoke | The upper back area between the shoulder blades, approximately 2–4 inches below the collar. Standard for small secondary logos or taglines. |
| Nape / back collar | The inside back collar area or the outside upper back neck. Used for brand labels, size tags, or subtle logo marks. |
| Sleeve | The outer or inner arm area, left or right. Common for team numbers, American flags, or secondary brand marks. |
| Hem | The bottom edge of the shirt front or back. Rare for primary logos, occasionally used for website URLs or small text. |

The left chest is the industry standard for B2B orders because it reads clearly in professional and field settings, works with both embroidery and screen printing, and scales across garment sizes without distorting.

### How Garment Size Affects Placement Dimensions

Placement measurements do not stay fixed across a size run. A logo positioned for an XL shirt will sit wrong on a Small. Placement must scale across the size run.

- **Youth and toddler sizes:** Scale all measurements down by 25–30%. A left chest logo on a youth medium measures 2.5–3" wide, not 3.5".
- **Women's cuts:** Place logos 1" higher than men's equivalents. Women's torso proportions shift the visual center upward. A left chest logo on a women's shirt sits 2–3" below the collar, not 3–4".
- **XL and above:** Add 0.5 inches to vertical drop per size above XL. A 3XL shirt receives a left chest logo 4.5–5" below the collar, not 3–4".
- **Bulk run handling:** NW Custom Apparel adjusts platen fixtures and registration marks per size during production. Buyers do not need to submit separate specs per size. We handle scaling automatically once the base measurement is approved.

However, when ordering a mixed-size run with others, specify placement for your median garment size (typically Medium or Large) and confirm the decorator scales across the run.

## Logo Placement by Garment Type

Different garments have different fabric structures, seam placements, and decoration constraints. See our guide for material selection, which fabric holds logo embroidery best.

Garment construction changes what's physically possible at each zone. A zip-up hoodie's front pocket blocks full-front placement. A polo's placket shifts left-chest positioning. Use this table before writing your spec sheet.

| Garment Type | Recommended Zones | Notes / Constraints |
| --- | --- | --- |
| T-shirt | Left chest, full front, full back, sleeve | Cotton and cotton-blend fabrics accept all decoration methods. Avoid center chest embroidery on thin jersey; puckering is common. |
| Polo shirt | Left chest, sleeve, nape | Pique knit and structured collars favor embroidery. Screen print on polo plackets produces uneven ink deposits. Left chest embroidery is the corporate standard. |
| Hoodie | Left chest, full front, full back, sleeve | Fleece pile absorbs more ink. Screen print requires lower mesh counts (86–110) for coverage. Left chest embroidery works on standard fleece; avoid heavy-weight sherpa. |
| Work jacket | Left chest, full back, sleeve, nape | Duck canvas and synthetic shells require embroidery or DTF. Screen print struggles on water-resistant coatings. Construction crew jackets often carry left chest company logo + full back crew name. |
| Long-sleeve button-down | Left chest, sleeve, nape | Pocket placement affects left chest positioning. Logos must clear pocket seams by 0.5" minimum. |
| Performance / athletic shirt | Left chest, sleeve, full back | Polyester and moisture-wicking fabrics require low-bleed plastisol or DTF. Standard plastisol risks dye migration above 300°F. |
| Hi-Vis safety shirt | Left chest, full back, sleeve | **Screen print only.** Embroidery hooping voids ANSI/ISEA 107-2015 certification by damaging retroreflective tape. See dedicated section below. |

## Screen Print vs Embroidery vs DTG vs DTF: How Decoration Method Changes Placement Rules

The decoration method determines where a logo can physically go, not just how it looks. Hoops have size limits. Platens have shape limits. DTG needs cotton. Choosing the wrong method for a placement zone generates reprints.

| Placement Zone | Screen Print Constraints | Embroidery Constraints | DTG Constraints | DTF Constraints |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Left chest | Max 4" × 4". Fine detail requires 200+ mesh. | Max 4" × 4". Minimum line width 0.125". Thin fonts stitch poorly. | Excellent for full-color logos. Max 4" × 4" on standard platens. | No fabric restrictions. Max 4" × 4". Works on polyester blends. |
| Center chest | Preferred method. Max 12" × 16". | Not recommended above 5" wide. Fabric puckers under large stitch counts. | Full-color gradients reproduce accurately. | Excellent for unlimited colors on any fabric. |
| Full front | Standard method. Underbase required on dark garments. | Impractical. Stitch count exceeds 50,000; production time and cost spike. | Best for cotton-rich garments. Polyester produces dull results. | Best method for polyester and mixed-fabric orders. No minimum. |
| Full back | Standard method. Large block text and team names print cleanly. | Possible up to 8" wide on stable fabrics. Exceeding this risks distortion. | Cotton only. Large print areas require pre-treatment. | Excellent for team jerseys and mixed-fabric orders. |
| Sleeve | Works on short and long sleeve. Placement must clear seams. | Cylinder arm frame required for cap sleeves. Structured cuffs limit access. | Small platen required. Limited to cotton and cotton blends. | No platen restrictions. Adheres to any sleeve fabric. |
| Nape / back collar | Small print area. Requires precise registration. | Tear-away backing required for inside placement to prevent skin irritation. | Difficult on curved seams. Pre-treatment complicates small areas. | Heat press application works on flat collar surfaces. |

Screen print deposits ink on top of fabric fibers. It struggles on structured areas like polo plackets, zipper covers, and thick fleece pile. The ink needs a flat, stable surface to achieve clean edges. For larger graphics on corporate shirts, [bulk screen printing for construction crews and event shirts](/services/screen-printing/) handles high-volume runs with consistent registration across the size run.

![Side-by-side visual comparison of screen printing versus embroidery on polo shirt fabric.](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/screen-print-vs-embroidery-comparison-1024x558.webp)

Choosing the right method: Screen printing offers fine detail, while embroidery provides a premium, professional texture. CHOOSE YOUR FINISH

Embroidery pushes thread through fabric. It cannot place dense stitch patterns near seams; the needle hits the seam fold and breaks or skips. This is why embroidery placement stays 0.5" minimum from any collar, placket, or sleeve seam. Washington businesses ordering [embroidered polo shirts and jackets](/services/custom-embroidery/) for corporate staff should confirm hoop clearance before finalizing placement.

DTG sprays water-based ink directly onto cotton fibers. It requires a flat platen and pre-treatment on dark garments. DTG does not work on polyester, spandex, or nylon. The ink sits on the surface rather than penetrating synthetic fibers. For full-color logos on 100% cotton shirts, [DTG printing for full-color logos on 100% cotton shirts](/direct-to-garment/) removes the screen setup fee.

DTF prints onto PET film and transfers with heat and adhesive. It has no fabric content restrictions. DTF works on cotton, polyester, nylon, canvas, spandex, and all blends. This makes DTF the only reliable method for construction crew performance shirts and athletic jerseys where fabric types vary across the order.

For polyester workwear and performance fabrics where DTG fails, [DTF printing for complex artwork on any fabric type](/services/direct-to-film-printing/) transfers cleanly without bleed or fading.

## Left Chest vs Full Front: Which Placement Works for Corporate Uniforms?

Left chest is the correct default for corporate uniforms. Full front works for event shirts, spirit wear, and retail-style apparel where brand visibility at distance matters more than professional subtlety. The right choice depends on context, not personal preference.

For example, A corporate office manager in Bellevue needs a uniform that reads professional in client meetings. A construction crew in Tacoma needs a shirt where the logo is visible at 30 feet on a job site. Those are different placement decisions.

| Use Case | Recommended Placement | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Daily corporate uniforms (office staff, client-facing teams) | Left chest | Standard for professional environments. Visible in seated meetings. Does not dominate the garment. |
| Trade show staff and event teams | Full front or center chest | Maximum brand visibility in crowded spaces. Photos and video capture the logo clearly. |
| Construction crew work shirts | Left chest (company) + full back (crew name) | Left chest identifies the company for safety and access. Full back identifies the crew or trade. |
| School staff and faculty | Left chest | Mirrors corporate uniform standards. Embroidered polo shirts are the K–12 standard across Washington state. |
| Sports team practice gear | Full front (team name) + left chest (sponsor) | Multiple placements carry different information. Full front dominates for team identity. |
| Volunteer and event giveaway shirts | Full front | Single large print delivers maximum brand exposure per dollar. Recipients wear these casually, not professionally. |
| Executive and management tiers | Left chest embroidery on premium blanks | Raised thread communicates quality. Premium polo shirts and button-downs with left chest embroidery distinguish leadership from general staff. |

For [corporate uniform orders for Washington State businesses](/services/staff-corporate-uniforms/), left chest embroidery on polos is the most common request. The left chest reads as authoritative without being loud.

## How to Specify Logo Placement When Ordering Custom Shirts in Bulk

Decorators do not guess placement. They execute from specs. If you do not provide a spec, most decorators default to left chest, center. That default may not match your brand standards, safety requirements, or garment construction. These six steps produce a spec any decorator can execute without back-and-forth.

1. **Choose placement zones from the standard zone list.** Reference the table in the "Standard Logo Placement Zones" section above. Select primary and secondary locations based on your use case.
2. **Confirm decoration method with your decorator.** Match the method to the garment and quantity. Embroidery for polo shirts and work jackets at 6+ pieces. Screen print for bulk T-shirts at 24+ pieces. DTG for small runs under 24 pieces. DTF for polyester and mixed-fabric orders with no minimum.
3. **Specify exact measurements.** Use the "Logo Placement Measurements" table above as your reference. State distance from collar seam in inches. State logo dimensions in inches, not pixels.
4. **Prepare artwork to correct dimensions and format.** Submit vector files (AI, EPS, PDF) for screen print and embroidery. Submit high-resolution PNG at 300 DPI minimum for DTG and DTF. Match logo dimensions to the recommended widths in the "Industry Standard Logo Sizes" table.
5. **Complete a placement spec sheet or tech pack.** See the checklist below for required fields. A complete spec sheet eliminates revision rounds and production delays.
6. **Request a pre-production sample (PPS) before full run.** NW Custom Apparel provides free pre-production samples on all bulk orders. Approve the sample in writing before full production begins. Changes after sample approval may restart setup.

### What to Include in a Placement Spec Sheet

For [school and team apparel orders in Washington State](/services/school-team-uniforms/), a complete spec sheet prevents the most common errors like wrong zone, size, and method. Include these eight items in every placement spec:

![Infographic checklist for creating a custom apparel logo spec sheet.](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/custom-apparel-spec-sheet-checklist-1024x558.webp)

- **Zone name** (left chest, full front, full back, sleeve, nape)
- **Distance from reference point** (inches from neckline seam or shoulder seam)
- **Logo dimensions** (width × height in inches at finished size)
- **Color callouts** (PMS numbers for screen print; thread color numbers for embroidery; CMYK values for DTG/DTF)

- **Garment style and color** (style number, colorway, and fabric content)
- **Mock-up approval confirmation** (note that you require a digital proof before production begins)

## Artwork File Requirements for Logo Placement

1. **Vector formats are required for screen print and embroidery.** Submit AI, EPS, or print-ready PDF files. Vector files scale without resolution loss and separate cleanly into individual color layers for screen exposure and stitch path generation.
2. **Raster files must meet minimum resolution.** High-resolution PNG or JPG at 300 DPI minimum at actual print size. Raster files require manual color separation before screen burning. Vector files eliminate this step and carry no artwork fee at NW Custom Apparel.
3. **Convert all text to outlines or curves.** Embedded fonts shift between computers. Outlined text locks the letterforms in place.
4. **Specify color mode by decoration method.** Screen print uses Pantone Solid Coated references. Embroidery uses thread color charts (Madeira or Isacord). DTG and DTF accept RGB files but convert to CMYK during production. Never submit RGB files for screen print or embroidery.
5. **Use clear file naming conventions.** Include company name, placement zone, and version number. Example: `AcmeCorp_LeftChest_v2.ai`. Avoid generic names like `logo_final.ai` or `image1.png`.

## Common Logo Placement Mistakes in B2B Orders

- **Assuming the decorator defaults to left chest without specifying.** Decorators have no default. Without written placement specs, your logo may land anywhere. Always state the zone, distance from seam, and dimensions in writing.
- **Submitting raster files instead of vectors.** A JPG logo blown up to 12 inches wide for a full back print pixelates and prints with jagged edges. Vector files maintain crisp edges at any size.
- **Not accounting for seam proximity with embroidery.** Embroidery needles break when they hit collar seams, placket folds, or sleeve hems. Keep embroidery placement 0.5" minimum from any seam line.
- **Ordering without a pre-production sample on multi-location placements.** A left chest + full back + sleeve order has three separate registration points. One misalignment multiplies across 100 pieces. Always approve a physical sample before full production.
- **Specifying logo size in pixels instead of inches.** Pixels describe screen resolution. Inches describe physical size. A decorator needs physical dimensions to set platen fixtures and screen frames. State width and height in inches.
- **Ignoring size run variation.** A logo positioned for an XL sample will sit too low on a Small and too high on a 3XL. Confirm with your decorator that placement scales across the full size run.
- **Using RGB colors for embroidery or screen print jobs.** RGB is a light-based color model for screens. Screen print ink mixes from Pantone formulas. Embroidery thread matches physical color charts. RGB values do not translate directly to either system.

## How Decorators Position Logos Consistently Across a Bulk Order

Consistency across 100, 500, or 1,000 pieces requires mechanical precision, not eyeballing. NW Custom Apparel uses three production controls at our Milton facility.

**Platens and fixtures** hold each garment in a fixed position during decoration. A left chest platen for adult T-shirts has a recessed pocket that centers the shirt at the correct distance from the collar. The operator loads the shirt the same way every time.

**Registration marks** align screens, embroidery hoops, or print heads to the same coordinates on every piece. A registration mark at the collar seam and a second at the side seam creates an X-Y coordinate system. The logo lands at the same intersection on garment 1 and garment 1,000.

**Digital placement templates** overlay the approved mock-up onto a photo of each garment type. Operators verify alignment against the template before production begins. This catches size scaling errors, seam interference, and orientation mistakes before ink hits fabric.

These controls matter for buyers because inconsistency is a quality control failure. A construction crew showing up to a job site with crooked logos looks unprofessional. A corporate team at a trade show with mismatched placement undermines brand standards. Mechanical registration eliminates these risks.

## Hi-Vis and Safety Shirt Placement: ANSI Rules for Washington Construction Crews

Washington construction crews on OSHA-regulated job sites must follow ANSI/ISEA 107-2015. This standard governs Hi-Vis apparel and directly affects logo placement.

**Screen print is the required decoration method on ANSI-certified Hi-Vis garments.** Embroidery hooping applies mechanical pressure to the retroreflective tape and creates permanent hoop marks. These marks void the ANSI certification. The garment can no longer be used on regulated job sites.

ANSI Class 2 certification requires a minimum of 775 square inches of background material and 201 square inches of retroreflective tape. Class 3 requires 1,240 square inches of background material and 310 square inches of retroreflective tape. Screen print must be applied to background fabric areas only. Placement cannot cover or interrupt any retroreflective tape segment.

## FAQs

**What size should a left chest logo be?**

A left chest logo should measure 3–3.5 inches wide and 3–3.5 inches tall. The maximum recommended size is 4 inches in either direction. This standard applies to men's, women's, and youth shirts with appropriate scaling.

**How far down from the collar should a logo be placed?**

A left chest logo sits 3–4 inches down from the collar seam. A center chest logo sits 2–3.5 inches down from the collar seam. A full front logo sits 3–4 inches down from the collar seam. All measurements are taken from the seam line, not the fabric edge.

**Can I add multiple logos to one shirt?**

Absolutely! Pair placements like a left chest logo with a back collar or sleeve design. Ensure the elements complement each other and don’t overcrowd the shirt.

**Can I embroider a Hi-Vis safety shirt?**

No. Embroidery hooping voids ANSI/ISEA 107-2015 certification by damaging retroreflective tape. Screen print is the required decoration method on Hi-Vis safety shirts. The print must be applied to background fabric only, avoiding all retroreflective tape segments.

**Does logo placement affect the cost of my order?**

Yes. Single-location placement (left chest only) is the baseline cost. Each additional location increases cost by approximately 60–80% of the first location's decoration charge. Multi-decoration methods (embroidery plus screen print on the same garment) require separate production passes and separate setup fees.

**What file format do I need for logo placement?**

Submit vector files (AI, EPS, or PDF) for screen print and embroidery. Submit high-resolution PNG or JPG at 300 DPI minimum for DTG and DTF. Convert all text to outlines. Specify Pantone colors for screen print and thread colors for embroidery.

**How do I specify placement for a bulk order?**

Write a placement spec sheet that includes: zone name, distance from reference seam in inches, logo dimensions in inches, color callouts, file format, decoration method, and mock-up approval confirmation. Request a pre-production sample before full production begins.
