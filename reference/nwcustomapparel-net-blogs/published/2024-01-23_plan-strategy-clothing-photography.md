# How to plan out strategy for Clothing Photography

Planning a photo shoot for your clothing brand can feel daunting. Without careful preparation, you risk wasting time, money, and creating images that don’t resonate with your audience. This delays product launches and hurt your brand's image, which leads to lost sales and engagement. However, by following a well-structured plan, you can capture stunning, brand-focused visuals that showcase your products and strengthen your brand identity. Let's guide you through each step to ensure a smooth, successful shoot.

## Developing a Strategic Plan for Your Clothing Brand Photography

Before picking up a camera, it's crucial to **strategically plan your photoshoot**. If you start a clothing brand, you’ll eventually need to promote it, and one of the best ways to do so is through showcasing the photos of your garments on models. A model provides the taste to your audience of how the product will look like on a real person compared to a static image of a shirt on a hanger.

To get the best results, think through every step of your strategy.

### Think of your brand

First, consider your **brand identity**—what values, mission, and goals do you want your visuals to reflect? Having a clear concept in mind of what your brand is, will help you make decisions like type of model and concept for the photo shoot. Whether you’re showcasing streetwear or high-end fashion, your brand identity should drive the concept of the shoot.

### What types of photos are you creating?

Next, decide what types of photos you're creating. Are they for a catalog, an online store, homepage, or an magazine? The answer will determine your shot styles, backgrounds, and angles. For example, photos for a catalogue or an online store are mostly shot against a white or neutral background in order not to distract from the clothing.

### What are the experts doing?

Look at the latest ad campaigns of famous fashion brands. What are they doing? What’s trending? Do you want to repeat them or go in a different direction? Research famous fashion photographers like **Mario Testino** and get acquainted with their work. The more research you do then the more resources you’ll have to produce your photo shoot.

### Create a look-and-feel and/or mood board

Consider creating a **mood board** to visualize your concept and share it with your team. Mood boards can be a very effective tool when it comes to visualising a project. They’re particularly helpful during the early stages when sometimes words are just not enough to flesh out what we have in our heads. Simply put, mood boards are a collection of images that represent a larger idea. The great thing about mood boards (aside from being useful) is that they’re easy to put together.

### Use the best model

Why should you hire a model? The model you’re going to choose is the person who will ultimately become the face of your brand (for a season, at least) so choose wisely, even if you’re working with a smaller budget. In a dream world, everyone would have enough budget to hire Cara Delevigne or Marlon Teixeira, but alas, most of the time this is not the case so we must do with the resources we have.

## 10 Steps to Plan a Clothing Brand Photo Shoot Like a Pro

### 1. Define Your Vision and Purpose

Start by **clarifying your brand’s identity**. Whether your brand is edgy, casual, or luxury-focused, make sure the visuals reflect this. Know who your target audience is and what kind of message you want to send. Are you shooting for social media, an online store, or an advertising campaign? Knowing the goal will help shape the entire photo shoot process. For example, if you're aiming for **social media engagement**, you may want vibrant, dynamic photos, while an e-commerce store might require **clean, product-focused shots**.

### 2. Choose the Right Photography Style

Choosing the **best photography style** depends on the message and vibe you want to convey. You could go for **product-only shots**, which are perfect for highlighting features like fabric texture, fit, and quality. Alternatively, opt for a **lifestyle shoot** to show your clothing in real-world settings, helping customers imagine themselves wearing your pieces. Brands like **Zara** mix **on-model shots** and product photos to reduce returns by giving customers a more accurate idea of fit and style.

Make sure to also consider **ghost mannequin photography** or **flat lays** if you're working with basics like t-shirts or casual wear.

### 3. Prepare a Detailed Shot List

A well-prepared shot list ensures that no product is overlooked. **List your outfits**, their colors, sizes, and the specific angles needed (e.g., front, back, side, or close-ups). This will keep your shoot organized and efficient. For example, for a product that features intricate embroidery, you may want **multiple close-ups** to highlight the detailing. Categorize the list by priority or how soon the photos are needed, especially if you have an upcoming launch.

### 4. Select and Coordinate Models

![select model for apparel photography](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/clothing-brand-photoshoot.webp)

The models you choose should reflect your brand’s **diversity** and target audience. For example, if your brand emphasizes inclusivity, select models with varied ethnicities, body types, and styles. Use a **casting call** or work with local agencies to find talent. Make sure the models are comfortable and aligned with your brand’s look and feel. This will make the photoshoot more cohesive and professional. Don't forget to schedule **fittings** beforehand to avoid any wardrobe malfunctions.

### 5. Secure the Right Location

Your location should complement your brand. **Studio shoots** provide controlled lighting and focus on the clothing, while **on-location** shoots add a lifestyle element. For luxury clothing, consider unique locations like **museums or galleries**, while streetwear might do well in **urban settings** like graffiti-covered walls or cityscapes. Make sure to obtain the necessary **permits** if you're shooting in public spaces.

### 6. Organize Props and Set Design

Props can elevate your clothing and help tell a story. Simple props like chairs, plants, or city bikes can add a **lifestyle feel** to your shoot. Just make sure they enhance, rather than distract from, the clothing. If you're shooting studio photos for e-commerce, a clean white or pastel background works best for emphasizing the **color and details** of your apparel.

Then you should consider **complementary items** that can complete a look. These include **accessories** such as shoes, bags, or jewelry, which help create a more cohesive visual. When planning your shoot, think about how these items will **complement** your main clothing pieces without overwhelming them. Including the right accessories can help customers imagine a complete outfit, which in turn can increase sales of not just clothing, but also **related items**.

### 7. Hire a Professional Photographer

The photographer is key to capturing your brand’s vision. Look for a photographer with experience in **fashion or product photography**. Check their portfolio to ensure their style matches what you’re looking for. For example, a photographer skilled in **high fashion** might excel at capturing intricate fabrics and dramatic lighting, while someone focused on **e-commerce** will know how to produce crisp, clean product images.

While hiring a skilled photographer is crucial, balancing quality with cost is essential. Always **discuss the budget upfront** and ensure both parties are clear on deliverables. **Negotiating** doesn’t just mean lowering costs; it could involve getting extra photos or quicker turnaround times. For smaller clothing brands, finding a **flexible photographer** who is willing to work within your budget without sacrificing quality can be a smart move. Some photographers might also offer packages, which can provide a more cost-effective solution for larger shoots.

### 8. Plan the Photoshoot Day

On the day of the shoot, make sure the **schedule is clear** to everyone involved. Block out time for **wardrobe changes**, styling adjustments, and breaks. Have a designated person managing the **shot list** to ensure nothing gets overlooked. If you're working with multiple models or stylists, coordination is crucial. Reminders the day before the shoot can ensure everyone is on the same page.

### 9. Post-Production and Editing

Post-production is where your photos come to life. This is the time to **edit the images** to ensure consistency. Adjust lighting, color, and contrast to keep everything aligned with your brand's aesthetic. For e-commerce photos, ensure **high resolution** images so customers can zoom in on the details. The quality of your images can reduce **returns** and increase conversions.

### 10. Distribute and Leverage Your Photos

A well-thought-out launch strategy is essential for maximizing the impact of your new photos. Instead of releasing all your images at once, stagger them across platforms like **social media, website banners, and email newsletters**. You could start by teasing some **behind-the-scenes shots** during the shoot to build excitement, then roll out the final images over a few weeks to keep your audience engaged. This gradual release helps ongoing interest in your brand and keeps customers anticipating the next product release.

## FAQ: How to Plan a Photo Shoot for Your Clothing Brand

**How much does it cost to plan a photo shoot for a clothing brand?**

The cost of a photo shoot varies based on factors like the number of models, location, photographer rates, and post-production. For a small to mid-sized brand, expect to spend anywhere from **$500 to $5,000**. This covers professional photography, models, studio rental, and any additional expenses like props and makeup artists. Larger brands with more elaborate shoots may have costs ranging up to **$10,000 or more**. Always get a quote upfront and negotiate for package deals to save costs.

**How long does it take to organize and execute a clothing photo shoot?**

Organizing a photo shoot typically takes **2 to 4 weeks**, depending on how quickly you can arrange logistics like securing a photographer, models, and location. The shoot itself usually lasts **1 to 2 days**, but can be extended if you’re shooting multiple collections or locations. Post-production (editing) may add another **1 to 2 weeks**, so it’s good to budget about a **month from planning to final images**.

**Do I need to hire professional models for my clothing photography?**

You don’t always need professional models, but it depends on your brand’s image. **Professional models** ensure high-quality results, especially for luxury or mid-market brands. However, **smaller or emerging brands** can often use friends, family, or **micro-influencers** to model their clothing. This can add a relatable element to your brand’s image and help save on costs. If you choose to hire professionals, consider using local modeling agencies for access to a pool of talent.

**How many outfits should I shoot in one photo session?**

The number of outfits depends on your collection size and the purpose of the shoot. On average, most brands shoot **10 to 30 outfits** in a single session. It’s important to have a detailed **shot list** to manage time efficiently and ensure that each outfit is captured from different angles. This ensures you get a variety of photos for use across multiple platforms like your website, social media, and lookbooks.

**What equipment is essential for a successful clothing photo shoot?**

Essential equipment for a successful clothing shoot includes a **high-resolution camera** (DSLR or mirrorless), **tripods**, **studio lighting** (softboxes, strobes), and **backdrops** (usually white or neutral). If shooting lifestyle photos outdoors, portable lighting like **reflectors** and **diffusers** will also help. It’s also useful to have a **laptop or tablet for tethered shooting** so you can review images in real-time and make adjustments on the spot.
