## The 8 Quality Signals Every Business Buyer Should Check

When a garment arrives embroidered, inspect it in good light such as daylight or strong indoor overhead lighting. Check these eight things in order.

### 1. Stitch Consistency

Every thread in the design should lie flat against the fabric at uniform tension. Run your finger across the design surface. You should feel a smooth, slightly raised texture with no bumps, gaps, or areas where threads loop or sag.

Uneven stitch consistency indicates a thread tension problem set during production. Loops on the surface mean tension is too loose. Tight, puckered areas mean tension is too high. Both are production calibration failures — not design issues, and not fixable after the fact.

### 2. Stitch Density - No Gaps, No Stiffness

Stitch density, the spacing between stitch lines is responsible for the compact embroidery look. Quality embroidery keeps stitch density between 0.4 and 0.45mm. If it is too low for the fabric, it leaves gaps and holes between stitches, letting the base cloth show through and detracting from the overall appeal.

The opposite failure is also visible: density set too high packs too many stitches into the fabric, producing a board-like feel and sometimes tearing the fabric at stress points. Feel the embroidered area on the back of the garment. A correctly densified design feels slightly raised but flexible. A stiff, rigid patch feeling means over-density.

Stitch count and density are directly connected to how large your logo runs at each placement. For a full breakdown by placement location, see the [embroidery stitch count guide for logo sizing and placement](/embroidery-stitch-count-guide/).

### 3. Text Legibility - Especially at Small Sizes

![](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/05/DSC01352-300x224.webp)

Hold the garment at arm's length. If your company name in your logo reads cleanly from that distance, text legibility passes. If it reads as a dense blob, the minimum text size was not respected in digitizing.

The industry minimum for readable satin stitch text is 4mm cap height. Below 4mm, satin stitches collapse into each other and individual letters lose their form. A quality embroiderer either increases text size slightly above 4mm or simplifies the design before production. An inferior shop runs it as-supplied and delivers illegible text.

### 4. Clean Borders and Edges

The outer edge of any shape in the design such as the border of a logo element, and the perimeter of a letter, should be sharp, not ragged. Threads should lay flat and move smoothly throughout the design without gaps, tangles, or uneven tension. Clean stitching ensures durability and enhances the clarity of every shape and letter.

A ragged or feathered edge on satin stitch columns indicates the column width was set too narrow for the stitch density, or the machine speed was too high for the fabric. Both produce thread displacement at the stitch edge.

### 5. No Trailing Threads or Untrimmed Jump Stitches

Turn the garment over and look at the underside. You may see connecting threads between design elements — these are jump stitches. In a quality production run, all jump stitches are trimmed on both the front and back of the garment before it leaves the machine. Any visible trailing thread on the design surface is a finish quality failure.

### 6. No Puckering Around the Design

Puckering is the most visible defect. It looks like fabric gathering or rippling around the embroidered area. Lay the garment flat on a table. The fabric around the design should lie completely flat. If it does not, the garment has puckered.

The root causes are explained in the digitizing section below. Puckering on a received order is not correctable. It is a production failure that requires reprinting.

### 7. Color Accuracy

Compare the embroidered thread colors against your brand color reference — your logo file, your brand guide, or a reference swatch. Thread colors should match your specified colors, not approximate them. Professional decorators use Madeira, Isacord, or equivalent commercial thread brands with documented Pantone-equivalent color systems, which allow your brand colors to be matched accurately.

![Monograms](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/05/Sub_Monogram-Button-300x165.webp)

Color that is noticeably different from what you specified — a navy that looks purple, a red that looks orange — indicates either the wrong thread color was pulled at production, or your decorator did not do a proper Pantone-to-thread match during the order setup.

### 8. Clean Backing on the Underside

Look at the underside of the embroidered area. You should see a backing material attached — either a soft woven cutaway backing or a tearaway paper backing. The backing should lie flat with no bunching. On knit fabrics like polo shirts, you should see a cutaway stabilizer (permanent, soft) rather than a tearaway (which leaves rough edges when removed from knit fabric).

If there is no backing at all, or if the backing is incorrectly chosen for the fabric type, the garment will not hold the embroidery correctly through washing and may pucker on the first laundry cycle.

## What Causes Puckering? And What It Tells You About Your Decorator

Puckering is the most common defect in commercial embroidery orders, and it is entirely preventable. When you receive an order with puckered garments, it tells you something specific about what went wrong in production.

**Cause 1: Missing or insufficient underlay stitches.** Underlay stitches are a foundation layer placed beneath the main design. They stabilize the fabric before the main stitches are applied. Without proper underlay, the main stitches pull the fabric as they are set, causing it to gather. A quality digitizer specifies the correct underlay type — edge-run, zigzag, or fill — based on the garment fabric and design shape. A shop that uses auto-digitizing software often skips or under-specifies underlay, which is why auto-digitized files produce more puckering than manually digitized ones.

**Cause 2: Wrong backing type for the fabric.** Knit fabrics (polo shirts, T-shirts, fleece) stretch under needle tension. They require a cutaway stabilizer — a permanent, woven backing that stays in the garment — to hold the design stable during production and through washing. Using a tearaway backing on a knit garment produces immediate puckering. Using no backing at all on knit is a production shortcut that fails on the first wear.

**Cause 3: Incorrect hooping tension.** The garment is clamped into a hoop frame before embroidery. If the fabric is stretched too tightly in the hoop, it relaxes when released and gathers around the design. Correct hooping places the garment snugly but not under tension.

**What this means for your order:** Puckering on a received order is the result of decisions made before the first stitch was placed. It cannot be fixed after production. If your current decorator is delivering puckered garments, the issue is in their digitizing or their production process — and the fix is not reprinting, it is finding a decorator who does both correctly.

## How Digitizing Quality Determines Everything Else

Most embroidery quality problems originate in the stitch file, not at the machine. Digitizing is the process of converting your logo into a machine-readable file that contains instructions for every needle penetration. A well-digitized file specifies the correct stitch type for each design element, the correct density for the fabric, appropriate underlay, and pull compensation — a built-in expansion of stitch paths that accounts for the fact that fabric shifts slightly under repeated needle penetration.

A poorly digitized file or one produced by auto-digitizing software without human review will produce puckering, misregistration, gaps, and illegible text regardless of how well-calibrated the embroidery machine is.

This is why digitizing is a one-time investment worth protecting. Once a logo is correctly digitized and approved through a physical sew-out, every subsequent order from that file produces the same quality result.

## How to Evaluate a Sew-Out Sample Before You Approve Production

A sew-out is a physical test stitch of your design on the actual garment style your order will use. It is produced before the full production run begins. NW Custom Apparel produces a sew-out on every new logo order and sends you a photo for approval before a single production piece is run.

Here is how to evaluate a sew-out photo:

1. **Check overall design shape.** Does it match your logo proportions? Digitizing sometimes adjusts elements for embroidery — confirm changes are acceptable before approval.
2. **Check text legibility.** Is every word and letter clearly readable? If text is unclear in the sew-out, it will not improve in production.
3. **Check thread colors.** Compare against your brand color reference. Request color corrections before approval, not after production.
4. **Check for puckering.** Ask for a photo that shows the fabric around the design, not just the design itself. Flat fabric around the design means correct underlay. Gathering means a production problem before it becomes your problem.
5. **Check the backing side.** Ask your decorator for a photo of the garment underside. Correct backing type should be visible.
6. **Check placement.** Confirm the logo position is exactly where you specified — left chest center, 4 inches from the collar — not approximated.

Approving a sew-out without reviewing these six points is the equivalent of approving a building plan without reading the specifications. Changes at the sew-out stage cost nothing. Changes after 200 garments are produced are expensive or impossible.

## Quality Differences by Garment Type

### Polo Shirts - Left-Chest Logo

The polo shirt is the most common corporate embroidery garment in Washington state. It is a knit fabric, which means it requires cutaway backing, appropriate underlay, and correctly set pull compensation. A left-chest logo on a polo should sit flat against the chest without pulling, gathering, or distorting the fabric underneath. The surrounding fabric should lie completely smooth.

Check the left chest area specifically: the horizontal chest seam near the armhole is a stress point during embroidery. If the design is placed too close to a seam, the hoop frame cannot stabilize the fabric properly and puckering appears at the seam edge. Quality decorators confirm placement relative to seam location before production.

### Caps - Curved Surface and Center Seam

![New Patch Caps](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/05/patch-cap-square-small-300x235.webp)

Cap embroidery is technically more demanding than flat garment embroidery, and the quality indicators are different. The center front seam of a structured cap creates a registration challenge — stitches must align perfectly on both sides of the seam. A quality cap embroidery file uses center-out stitching sequence, starting from the design center and working outward, to prevent fabric from bunching against the seam as stitches accumulate.

On a received cap order, inspect the design on both sides of the center seam. The design should appear continuous, with no offset, gap, or misregistration at the seam line. Any visible misalignment at the seam is a cap driver or digitizing problem, not a machine error.

### Work Jackets - Duck Canvas and Heavy Fabrics

![](https://nwcustomapparel.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/05/ILWUcapfront-298x300.webp)

12-ounce duck canvas work jackets require the heaviest underlay, the largest needle gauge, and a cutaway backing that is matched to the canvas weight. The fabric's tight, rigid weave resists needle penetration more than knit or woven shirt fabric. A quality left-chest logo on a canvas jacket should produce a clean, raised design with crisp edges. The duck canvas around the design should show no puckering or hoop marks.

For large back yoke embroidery on jackets, a 10–14 inch design with the company name, registration across the full width of the design is the primary quality check. All letters should sit at a consistent baseline with uniform spacing and consistent satin stitch column width from the first letter to the last.

## Thread Quality: Commercial Grade vs Inferior Thread

The thread used in your order matters for color accuracy, durability, and appearance. Commercial decorators use 40-weight polyester thread from manufacturers like Madeira or Isacord. These threads have consistent dyeing, tight construction, high tensile strength, and documented colorfastness ratings — colors do not bleed, fade, or shift significantly through standard commercial washing.

Inferior threads (unbranded or low-cost generic threads) produce inconsistent colorfastness, higher thread breakage rates during production (which causes production stops and potential defects), and sheen differences that make thread colors look different on garment than they appeared in the proof.

Ask your decorator which thread brand they use. A shop that cannot name their thread brand is likely using generic thread.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the most important sign of good embroidery quality?**

Stitch consistency is the most visible and reliable quality indicator. Every thread should lie flat at uniform tension across the entire design with no loops, gaps, or bunching. Inconsistent stitching affects both appearance and durability regardless of design complexity.

**What stitch density produces the best embroidery quality?**

Commercial embroidery quality uses a stitch density between 0.4 and 0.45mm between stitch lines. Below 0.4mm, gaps appear between stitches and the base fabric shows through. Above 0.45mm, the design becomes rigid and can damage the fabric at stress points.

**What causes embroidery to pucker?**

Puckering is caused by three production failures: insufficient or missing underlay stitches beneath the main design, wrong backing type for the fabric (especially tearaway on knit fabrics), or incorrect hooping tension. All three are preventable at the digitizing and setup stage. Puckering cannot be corrected after production.

**What backing should be used on polo shirts?**

Polo shirts are knit fabrics and require a cutaway stabilizer — a permanent woven backing that remains in the garment. Tearaway backing on a polo shirt leaves rough edges after removal and does not stabilize the knit fabric adequately during production, resulting in puckering.

**What is the minimum text size for readable embroidery?**

The minimum readable text height for satin stitch embroidery is 4mm. Below 4mm, the satin stitches collapse together and individual letter shapes become indistinct. For logos with small text elements, a quality decorator will confirm minimum size before digitizing and flag any text that cannot be accurately reproduced at the intended size.

**How do I know if my embroidery decorator is using quality thread?**

Ask directly: which thread brand do they use? Quality commercial decorators use Madeira, Isacord, Robison-Anton, or equivalent brands with documented Pantone color systems and colorfastness ratings. Shops that cannot name their thread brand are likely using generic or unbranded thread.

**Should I always ask for a physical sew-out before approving my order?**

Yes, for any new logo or new garment type. A sew-out on the actual garment — not a digital mockup — is the only reliable way to confirm thread color accuracy, design legibility, puckering risk, and placement before committing to a full production run.
