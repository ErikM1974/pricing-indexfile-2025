# Screen Printing Artwork: The Industrial Guide to Press-Ready Design

In professional garment decoration, the quality of the final product is decided long before the squeegee hits the mesh. It is decided in the **pre-press phase**. For many brand owners and graphic designers, the biggest point of friction is understanding that a "good-looking" file on a high-resolution monitor is not necessarily a "press-ready" file for an automatic screen printing machine.

At Northwest Custom Apparel, we see thousands of designs annually. The #1 cause of production delays and unexpected art charges is improperly prepared artwork. This guide establishes the technical bridge between digital creativity and industrial reality, ensuring your brand’s vision is executed with surgical precision.

## Why Your Digital File Isn't "Press-Ready" Yet

When you view a design on a laptop, you are looking at light-emitting pixels in an **RGB (Red, Green, Blue)** color space. Screen printing, however, is a physical process involving **pigment-based inks** pushed through a woven mesh.

A "Press-Ready" file must account for:

- **Physical Separation:** Every color in your design requires its own physical screen.
- **Ink Opacity:** Standard inks are translucent; they require a "base plate" to appear vibrant on dark fabrics.
- **Mechanical Tolerance:** Machines have a "registration" limit. If your lines are too thin or your colors are too close without "trapping," the print will fail.

By mastering the technical requirements below, you move from being a t-shirt designer to an **apparel production specialist**.

Once your files are finalized to these industrial standards, our production team utilizes them for high-volume **[custom screen printing shirts](https://nwcustomapparel.net/services/screen-printing/)** to ensure every garment in your order is identical.

## The Vector Gold Standard: Why Geometry Beats Pixels

The most critical distinction in screen printing artwork is the difference between **Vector** and **Raster** graphics. For logos, typography, and "flat" illustrations, Vector is the undisputed king.

### The Physics of Vector Files (.AI, .EPS, .SVG)

Vector graphics are not made of pixels; they are made of mathematical paths—points, lines, and curves. Because these shapes are calculated in real-time by the software (like Adobe Illustrator), they possess **infinite scalability**.

- **Resolution Independence:** You can scale a 2-inch vector logo to fit a 40-foot billboard, and the edges will remain perfectly crisp.
- **Ease of Separation:** In a vector file, a "Red" shape is a distinct mathematical object. Our pre-press team can select that object and instantly assign it to a physical screen.

**The "Upsampling" Trap:** A common mistake is taking a low-resolution .JPG from a website and saving it as a .PDF or .EPS. This does *not* create vector art. It simply wraps a "raster" image in a "vector" container. To be true vector art, the design must be reconstructed using paths.

Professional designers typically use industry-standard tools like Adobe Illustrator to ensure **[vector artwork specifications](https://helpx.adobe.com/illustrator/using/drawing-basics.html)** meet the crisp geometry required for physical mesh exposure.

## Mastering Raster Art for High-Detail Printing

While vectors are ideal for logos, **Raster art** (.PSD, .TIFF) is necessary for photographs, complex paintings, and designs with heavy texture or shading. Raster images are built on a grid of colored squares called pixels.

### The 300 DPI Mandate

In screen printing, Resolution is everything. We require a minimum of **300 DPI (Dots Per Inch)** at the **final print size**.

Consider the math:

- If you want a 12-inch wide back print, your digital file must be **3,600 pixels wide** (12 inches x 300 DPI).
- If you provide a 72 DPI image (the standard for web viewing), that 12-inch print will appear jagged, blurry, and unprofessional.

### Lossless vs. Lossy Formats

Never use **JPEGs** for high-end production if possible. JPEGs use "lossy" compression, which creates "artifacts"—small blurs around the edges of your design. Instead, provide **layered Photoshop (.PSD) files** or **Tagged Image File Format (.TIFF)** files. Layered files allow us to isolate the design from the background, which is essential for printing on anything other than a white t-shirt.

## The Science of Color: Spot Colors vs. CMYK

How we translate your digital colors into physical ink is the most technical part of the process. There are three primary methods used in industrial shops.

### The Pantone Matching System (PMS)

For 90% of screen printing, we use **Spot Colors**. We don't mix colors on the shirt; we mix them in a bucket before they go on the press.

- **The Advantage:** If your brand uses "Coca-Cola Red," we use the specific Pantone formula to ensure that red is identical across 5,000 shirts.
- **Pre-Press Requirement:** Assign your vector shapes to specific **Pantone Solid Coated** swatches.

### Halftones: The Illusion of Gradients

If your design has a fading effect or a shadow, we use **Halftones**. These are tiny dots of varying sizes. From a distance, the human eye blends these dots into a smooth gradient.

High-mesh screens (230 to 305 threads per inch) are required to hold these tiny dots. If your art has too many fine gradients, it may require a "Simulated Process" approach.

### CMYK (Process) vs. Simulated Process

- **CMYK:** Uses four translucent inks (Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, Black) to create a full spectrum. This works best on **White garments only**.
- **Simulated Process:** The professional choice for printing "photographic" images on **Black or Navy shirts**. It uses a series of opaque spot colors (often 6 to 10 screens) to recreate a full-color image with incredible vibrancy.

To maintain total brand consistency across different global suppliers, we rely on the **[Pantone Matching System](https://www.pantone.com/color-systems/pantone-color-systems-explained)**, which provides a universal language for spot color identification.

## Technical Prep: Converting Your Vision to Screens

Before a file can be sent to our **CTS (Computer-to-Screen)** imaging system, it must undergo a final technical sanitization. This is where most amateur designs fail.

### Turning Text into Outlines

Even if you provide a PDF or AI file, if we do not have your specific font installed on our pre-press stations, the software will substitute it with a generic font like Arial.

**So,** Always select your text and use the **"Create Outlines"** (Adobe Illustrator) or **"Convert to Curves"** (CorelDRAW) command. This turns your letters into mathematical shapes that remain consistent on every computer in the world.

### Stroke Weights and Line Minimums

Ink has physical volume. If your design features lines that are too thin, the ink will physically clog the mesh of the screen, or the line will "blow out" during the screen-burning process.

- **The Standard:** We recommend a minimum line thickness of **1pt** (or 0.014 inches). For negative space (gaps between colors), increase this to **1.5pt** to prevent the ink from bleeding together.

### The Essential White Underbase

When printing on dark garments (Black, Navy, Forest Green), the fabric fibers naturally soak up ink, making colors look dull or muddy.

We print a **White Underbase** first. Think of this as a primer on a wall. We then "flash dry" this layer and print your colored inks on top.

**Pro Tip:** In your artwork file, you don't need to create this underbase yourself; our pre-press software handles it. However, you should be aware that highly detailed "fades" into a dark shirt color are much harder to achieve with an underbase.

If your design features ultra-fine details on polyester or difficult blends, you may want to consult our **[DTF printing TCO and ROI business guide](https://nwcustomapparel.net/dtf-printing-tco-roi-business-guide/)** for an alternative to heavy white underbases.

## Industrial Pre-Press at Northwest Custom Apparel

When you submit your **screen printing artwork** to our Milton, WA facility, it enters a rigorous seven-step industrial workflow designed for maximum efficiency and color accuracy.

1. **Art Review:** Our designers check for resolution, line weight, and font outlines.
2. **Color Separation:** We break your file down into individual monochromatic layers (one for each ink color).
3. **CTS Imaging:** Using direct-to-screen technology, we use lasers or wax to "image" your design directly onto emulsion-coated screens—eliminating the need for old-fashioned film positives.
4. **Exposure & Washout:** The screens are exposed to UV light, hardening the emulsion everywhere except where your design is. We then wash away the soft emulsion, leaving a perfect stencil.
5. **Press Setup (Registration):** Our operators align the screens on an automatic press. This is where "Registration Marks" are vital; they ensure that the red ink hits exactly where the blue ink ends.
6. **Production Run:** We print a "Test Strike" for quality approval before running the full bulk order.
7. **Curing:** Every shirt passes through a conveyor dryer at **320°F** to "plastisolize" the ink, ensuring it will never crack or peel.

## FAQs: Screen Printing Artwork

**How to prepare a photo for screenprint?**

Photos must be converted into Halftones. This process turns the photograph into thousands of tiny dots. For best results, send us the original high-resolution photo, and our art department will handle the halftone conversion to match our specific screen mesh counts.

**Can I use a design from Canva or Inkscape?**

Yes, but you must export them correctly. From **Canva**, export as a "PDF Print" and ensure "Flatten PDF" is NOT checked. From **Inkscape**, save as an .SVG or .EPS to maintain vector paths.

**What are the 7 steps of screen printing?**

The seven steps are: (1) Art Creation, (2) Screen Preparation, (3) Ink Mixing, (4) Press Setup/Registration, (5) Printing/Squeegee Stroke, (6) Heat Curing, and (7) Quality Control.

**How do I prepare artwork for a 4T shirt?**

Scale your design down to a maximum of 6" width. Because toddler shirts have very little surface area, avoid Full Front designs that wrap around the sides of the ribs, as this makes the shirt uncomfortable to wear.
