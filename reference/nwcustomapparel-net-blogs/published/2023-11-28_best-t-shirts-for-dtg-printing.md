# Best Shirts for DTG Printing: How Fabric Choice Affects Print Quality

## The Fabric Dilemma in DTG Printing

If you’ve ever [printed a custom t-shirt](https://nwcustomapparel.net/services/custom-t-shirt-printing/) only to find the design fading after a few washes, the issue isn’t necessarily the printer—it’s likely the fabric. Direct-to-garment printing is an advanced technique that applies water-based ink directly onto fabric fibers. While it allows for **photorealistic, full-color prints**, not all shirts are designed to hold that level of detail.

Choosing the wrong fabric can lead to dull prints, ink bleeding, or quick fading, while the right fabric ensures sharp, vibrant designs that last through washes. To achieve the best results, several key factors come into play:

- **Fabric type:** 100% cotton absorbs ink better than polyester.
- **Weave quality:** A smoother weave means crisper details.
- **Pre-treatment:** Required for dark colors and polyester blends.
- **Ink absorption:** Fabrics that repel ink result in faded prints.

This article breaks down **the best shirts for DTG printing**, compare top brands, and explore how to prep shirts for maximum print quality.

## How DTG Printing Works & Why Fabric Matters

DTG printing works like an advanced inkjet printer for fabric, using water-based inks that embed into the fibers instead of sitting on top like traditional screen printing or heat transfers. This allows for soft, highly detailed prints that don’t crack or peel.

However, the effectiveness of DTG printing depends entirely on the fabric. If a fabric **absorbs ink well**, the colors will appear vibrant and sharp. If the fabric **repels ink**, the result is a washed-out, blurry print. This is why not all shirts are ideal for DTG printing—some fabrics require special treatments to improve ink adhesion.

### Fabric Challenges in DTG Printing

- **Cotton absorbs ink well**, producing sharp, bright prints.
- **Polyester repels water-based ink**, making prints look faded unless pre-treated.
- **Tri-blends (cotton/poly/rayon) have a soft feel but struggle with ink absorption.**

The key to achieving durable, high-quality DTG prints is selecting the right material for your design needs.

## Best Fabrics for DTG Printing

Different fabrics react differently to DTG inks, affecting color vibrancy, print durability, and softness. Here's a closer look at how popular fabric types perform:

### 100% Combed & Ring-Spun Cotton – The Gold Standard

Cotton is the **best choice for DTG printing**, but not all cotton is equal. Basic cotton fabrics can have loose fibers that interfere with print clarity. Instead, combed and ring-spun cotton is ideal because it has a tighter weave and a smoother surface, which helps ink adhere evenly for vivid, detailed prints.

**Pros:**  
✔ High ink absorption for bright, bold prints  
✔ Soft, breathable, and comfortable  
✔ Durable when washed properly

**Cons:**  
✖ Regular (non-ring-spun) cotton may have rough fibers, affecting print quality

### Cotton-Polyester Blends – A Tradeoff Between Durability & Print Quality

Cotton-poly blends combine the softness of cotton with the strength of polyester. While they are more durable and less prone to shrinking, they don’t absorb ink as well as pure cotton, making prints look slightly faded.

**Pros:**  
✔ More durable and moisture-wicking  
✔ Feels softer than pure cotton

**Cons:**  
✖ Prints are less vibrant unless pre-treated

> **Best Shirts in This Category:** Next Level 6210 CVC, BELLA+CANVAS 3413 Tri-Blend

### Tri-Blends – Fashionable but Not DTG-Friendly

Tri-blend shirts mix cotton, polyester, and rayon, creating a super-soft, vintage-feel fabric. However, rayon doesn’t absorb ink well, making DTG prints look more muted than on cotton.

**Pros:**  
✔ Extremely soft, lightweight, and trendy

**Cons:**  
✖ Prints won’t be as sharp or vibrant

> **Best Shirts in This Category:** BELLA+CANVAS 3413, American Apparel Tri-Blend

### 100% Polyester – The Most Difficult Fabric for DTG

Polyester is great for athletic and moisture-wicking apparel, but it repels water-based ink, making DTG printing very challenging. Without heavy pre-treatment, ink won’t bond properly, leading to quick fading and poor adhesion.

**Pros:**  
✔ Durable and moisture-resistant

**Cons:**  
✖ Poor ink absorption without pre-treatment

**Alternative:** Sublimation printing works better for 100% polyester shirts.

## FAQs

**What thread count is best for DTG printing?**

For DTG printing, shirts with a higher thread count work best because they provide a smoother surface for ink adhesion. The ideal range is 30s to 40s (single count), meaning the fabric is finer and more tightly woven.

**Can you print on textured or ribbed fabric with DTG?**

DTG printing works best on smooth, flat surfaces. Textured or ribbed fabrics create uneven ink distribution, leading to blurry designs and inconsistent color absorption.

**Do darker shirts require special treatment for DTG printing?**

Yes, dark-colored shirts require pre-treatment before DTG printing. Unlike light shirts, where ink absorbs naturally, DTG inks (especially white ink) need a bonding layer to sit properly on dark fabrics.

**How do DTG-printed shirts compare to screen-printed shirts in durability?**

Durability depends on fabric type, ink quality, and washing practices.
