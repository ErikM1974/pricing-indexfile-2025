# How to Set Up a Company Uniform Program: Step-by-Step Guide for WA Businesses

Setting up a company uniform program for the first time feels complicated because it is. You are making decisions about garment style, logo placement, decoration method, sizing, distribution, and reorder logistics; often without a clear framework for which decision comes first.

This guide gives Washington businesses that framework, built on 48 years of producing uniform programs for construction crews, trade companies, corporate teams, and service businesses across the Pacific Northwest. It also covers what most uniform guides skip entirely: what Washington state law actually requires employers to pay for.

## What Is a Company Uniform Program?

A company uniform program is a structured system for selecting, branding, ordering, distributing, and replacing employee apparel on a repeatable schedule. It goes beyond a one-time order. A program defines which employees wear what, how new hires get their uniforms, how replacements are handled, and who controls the ordering process.

Washington businesses that run a program — rather than placing ad-hoc orders whenever someone calls — spend less per garment, maintain a consistent branded appearance across their crew, and eliminate the administrative overhead of managing individual uniform requests from employees and managers.

## Step 1: Define What the Program Needs to Accomplish

Before selecting a single garment, answer three questions:

**What is the primary purpose?** Washington construction companies build programs around safety compliance and crew identification. Corporate and professional services businesses build them around brand consistency and client impression. Trade companies (HVAC, electrical, plumbing) often need both: hi-vis compliant shirts for field crews and branded polo shirts for office and estimating staff.

**Who will wear uniforms?** Map your employee roles to garment requirements. Field crew members working near traffic need ANSI-compliant hi-vis shirts and jackets. Office staff need polo shirts or work shirts. Supervisors often wear a different garment from field crew to signal role differentiation — the same logo, a different style.

**How often will the program need to produce uniforms?** Companies with stable headcounts order annually. Washington construction and HVAC companies with regular crew turnover need a program that can produce individual garments for new hires within 5–10 business days, not in bulk runs only.

## Step 2: Understand Washington State Employer Uniform Law

This step is missing from every other uniform program guide. It matters for Washington employers.

Washington state law governs when employers must pay for employee uniforms. The relevant statute is **RCW 49.12.450**, enforced by [Washington L&I](https://www.lni.wa.gov/workers-rights/workplace-policies/uniforms).

The law is specific. Required clothing is considered a uniform, and must be paid for entirely by the employer. If it clearly identifies the employee with a specific employer, has the employer's logo, is a clothing item of an uncommon color, has an ethnic or historical theme, or is formal clothing such as tuxedos or gowns. Employers may not deduct from an employee's wages or require a deposit for a uniform.

What this means in practice for Washington businesses:

**You must pay for:** Any garment with your company logo embroidered or screen printed on it. Any garment in a non-standard color your company requires. Safety apparel required for the job (this is also covered separately under OSHA's PPE payment requirement).

**You are not required to pay for:** General dress code requirements met through common colors. If an employer requires employees to wear common colors — white, tan, or blue tops and tan, black, blue, or gray bottoms — that conform to a general dress code, the employer is not required to furnish or compensate employees for that apparel.

**You may not:** Deduct the cost of a logo uniform from an employee's wages. Require employees to put down a deposit for a logo uniform. Per Washington L&I Administrative Policy ESC 8.1, if the required clothing is a uniform, the employer must absorb the full cost.

For multi-location Washington businesses or companies with employees in multiple states, federal FLSA Uniform Law also applies. Under the FLSA, if a uniform is required by the employer, by law, or by the nature of the job, then the uniform is considered a benefit to the employer and this expense is covered by the employer — including not just the cost of the uniform but laundering and maintenance fees.

Document your uniform policy in your employee handbook. Washington requires specific state policies in employee handbooks, and while a uniform policy is not mandated, documenting it in writing protects both the employer and the employee in a dispute.

## Step 3: Identify Garment Needs by Role and Environment

Match garment type to each role in your organization. Washington businesses typically map roles into two or three tiers:

**Tier 1 - Field crew / trade workers:**

- [Hi-vis safety shirts (ANSI Class 2 or Class 3 depending on traffic exposure)](https://nwcustomapparel.net/services/custom-hi-vis-shirts/)
- Work shirts or T-shirts in crew colors for non-road-adjacent work
- Insulated work jackets or hi-vis jackets for outdoor winter work
- Embroidered caps — the highest-visibility branded item a field worker wears daily

**Tier 2 - Supervisors and project managers:**

- Embroidered polo shirts
- Embroidered work jackets
- Embroidered caps (same design as crew, different garment for role differentiation)

**Tier 3 - Office and estimating staff:**

- Polo shirts or dress shirts with left-chest logo
- Optional branded outerwear for client-facing meetings and site visits

## Step 5: Select Garments and Decoration Method

### Garment Selection

Work with your supplier to narrow garment selection for each tier. Variables to address before ordering:

**Fabric and weight for the work environment.** A HVAC technician crawling through attic spaces needs a different fabric weight than a corporate account manager attending client meetings. Field crew in Washington's wet winter months need moisture-wicking or insulated outer layers.

**Color selection within your brand.** Your company colors may or may not be available in the garment styles you want. Wholesale networks like SanMar carry 100+ colors on most polo and work shirt styles, but specific brand colors may require PMS thread matching for embroidery or Pantone color separation for screen printing.

**Extended sizing.** Washington construction crews include workers from XS through 5XL or 6XL. Confirm size availability before finalizing the garment selection. Some styles are available through 6XL with a small upcharge on 2XL and above, which is industry standard.

### Decoration Method

**Embroidery** is the standard for polo shirts, work shirts, caps, and jackets where a left-chest logo is the primary brand mark. It is durable, professional, and survives years of industrial washing without fading. The one-time digitizing fee applies to new designs, then all reorders use the same stored file at no additional cost. See [custom embroidered work jackets for Washington construction crews](https://nwcustomapparel.net/custom-embroidered-jackets/).

**Screen printing** is the standard for T-shirts, hi-vis shirts, hoodies, and large-format graphics. It is more cost-effective than embroidery for high-volume runs and handles safety stripe applications and oversized designs that embroidery does not.

For most Washington trade and construction programs, the combination is: embroidery for caps, polo shirts, and jackets; screen printing for T-shirts, hi-vis shirts, and hoodies.

## Step 6: Submit Your Logo, Approve the Sew-Out, Confirm Sizing

**Logo submission.** Submit the highest-quality version of your logo — AI or EPS vector files produce the best results for both embroidery digitizing and screen printing separation. If you only have a JPG, your supplier's art team can work with it, but expect a longer prep time.

**Sew-out approval.** Before any production run begins, NW Custom Apparel produces a physical sew-out on the actual garment style your program uses. You receive a photo for review. This step catches color accuracy issues, stitch density problems, and placement errors before they affect 50 or 200 garments. Approve the sew-out before the run starts. This is non-negotiable.

**Size collection.** Collect individual size preferences from all employees before placing the order. For programs with 50+ employees, a simple form distributed via email or your HR platform captures sizes efficiently. Include a size range chart from your garment supplier so employees know what size to request. For hi-vis garments specifically, the January 2025 OSHA PPE fit rule requires that garments properly fit each individual worker — shared sizing bins are not acceptable.

## Step 7: Choose Your Distribution Method

### Option 1: Bulk Delivery to One Location

For Washington businesses with all employees in one location, bulk delivery to your facility is the simplest distribution method. You receive the full order, distribute garments by employee, and store spares for new hires and replacements. This works well for stable crews of 10–50 employees.

### Option 2: Branded Company Webstore

For Washington businesses with multiple locations, distributed crews, or frequent new hire onboarding, a company webstore eliminates the administrative burden of managing individual uniform requests.

## Step 8: Build Reorder and Replacement Into the Program From Day One

The most common uniform program failure is not the initial launch, but the reorder process 12 months later when the first crew member's shirt is worn out or a new hire starts and has no uniform.

Build these elements into your program at launch:

**Replacement schedule.** Washington construction and trade companies typically replace field crew garments every 12–18 months. Hi-vis shirts replace on a faster cycle — daily-use garments should be inspected and replaced when retroreflective tape degrades, typically at 6–12 months. Set a calendar reminder and a budget line for replacement before the first order ships.

**New hire onboarding.** Decide before launch how new employees receive their uniforms. Options include: a standing inventory of common sizes at your facility, a webstore new-hire allowance that activates on day one of employment, or a standard reorder with NW Custom Apparel using the stored logo file (no repeat setup fee, turnaround 10–14 business days).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does a Washington employer have to pay for employee uniforms?**

Yes, if the garment qualifies as a uniform under RCW 49.12.450. Any garment with the employer's logo, an uncommon color required by the employer, or distinctive markings that identify the employee with a specific employer must be paid for entirely by the employer. Washington L&I prohibits deducting uniform costs from wages or requiring employee deposits.

**What garments do not qualify as uniforms under Washington law?**

Garments in common colors like white, tan, or blue tops; tan, black, blue, or gray bottoms, that meet a general dress code but are not specific to the employer do not qualify as uniforms. Employees can be required to wear these without employer reimbursement.

**How many garments should each employee receive in a uniform program?**

For field crew and trade workers, a minimum of 3 shirts and 2 caps per employee provides enough rotation for a standard work week without daily laundering. For employees who require hi-vis compliance, 3 hi-vis shirts plus 2 standard shirts for non-traffic-adjacent work is a common configuration. Jackets are typically issued one per employee annually.

**What is the difference between a bulk delivery and a company webstore?**

Bulk delivery sends the full order to one location. A company webstore allows each employee to self-order their garments and sizes directly through a branded online portal, with admin controls for spend limits and approval. Webstores work best for Washington businesses with 30+ employees, multiple locations, or regular new hire turnover.

**How long does it take to get a new hire's uniform after the program is set up?**

Once your logo is digitized and the program is established, reorders ship within 10–14 business days from order confirmation. With a company webstore and pre-stocked inventory, new hire uniforms can be available within 5–7 business days.

**Do I pay a digitizing fee on every reorder?**

No. NW Custom Apparel stores your approved stitch file and applies it to every reorder at no additional digitizing cost. The digitizing fee is one-time per logo. For orders of 48 or more caps, the initial digitizing fee is waived entirely.

**Should I include hi-vis shirts in my uniform program?**

Yes, if any employees work near moving vehicle traffic. Washington employers are required under WAC 296-155-200 to provide ANSI-compliant hi-vis outer garments to workers near vehicles. This is also a safety PPE obligation under OSHA, which means employer-paid regardless of the uniform law analysis. Include hi-vis shirts in Tier 1 of your program and set a 6–12 month replacement schedule for daily-use garments.
