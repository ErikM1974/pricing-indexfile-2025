Why this role matters

"When UPS drops off a load of boxes, they aren't cartons of shirts — they're boxes of money."

Every shirt that is unaccounted for, missing, or the wrong style or color costs the company money. Receiving's number-one job is to prevent that financial loss — and the clock is always ticking, because production needs to start soon. Anything received today is counted today. No exceptions.

Shipping is the last checkpoint. If every department does its job perfectly but shipping misses the date, the method, or the address, we fumbled the ball on the one-yard line. Jobs go out on time, by the right method, to the right address — that's the whole game.

Golden rule: When you're unsure, ask — never guess. A miscount or a lost shirt is lost money, and that's the part of this job that matters most.

Step-by-step: receiving a garment shipment

  1. Sort freight by PO. Print the PSST daily manifest from SanMar. Find the PO number on each box and group boxes with the same PO together. Verify the box count against the PO (e.g., 10 of 10 boxes arrived).
  2. Pull the paperwork. Open the PO in ShopWorks (the PO number is on the packing slip). Print the work order and the box labels — you'll need them in the next steps.
  3. Check in the shipment. Remove the packing slip. Cut the top of the box first, then the sides, to avoid cutting the contents; dump from the bottom so items stay stacked. Sort by style and color, and count quickly (dozens for t-shirts, sixes for hoodies).
  4. Verify the garments. Confirm the packing slip, the PO, and the actual garments all agree on style, color, and size. Highlight verified lines on the work order so finished checks are obvious.
  5. Mark received in ShopWorks. Put the garments back in the box and change the PO status from Ordered → Received (or partially received). Note any missing pieces on the order's front/main note page. Print the box label. Do NOT put the work order inside the box.
  6. Stage for production. Whoever counts initials and dates the packing slip. Move the box to the embroidery / DTG staging area, staged by the last digit of the work order number. Lay hoodies face-down (they print last) so fuzz doesn't transfer onto the t-shirts. Attach the work order + box label to the stack.

Box labels & partial orders

Print a box label and stick it on the upper-left corner of the short side of the box. Every label shows: work order #, ship date, order name, and client name.

  • Complete: mark complete in ShopWorks, label the box, stage in the production inventory area by the last digit of the work order #.
  • Partial: mark partial and note what's missing. Write a big black "P" on the label with a fat marker and stage it in the receiving partial inventory area (by last digit). When the rest arrives, add it to the box, update ShopWorks, print a fresh label over the "P", and move it to production.
A partial can go straight to production if the remainder will arrive within 2–3 business days (check the SanMar site for where/when the rest ships; ask the purchasing clerk for other vendors). Otherwise it waits on the partial shelf.

Discrepancies & no-PO boxes

  • Wrong or extra item? Keep receiving the rest, set the incorrect item aside in the designated return space (this is a partial receive), and note the missing garment on the front page of the work order.
  • After the full UPS shipment is in, tell the purchasing clerk (Bradley) about the incorrect garments. Purchasing reorders and gets an RA number + label.
  • No PO on a box? Don't receive it. Isolate it in the holding area and notify Bradley. Nothing goes into stock without a matching ShopWorks PO.
  • Damaged in transit? Photograph everything and notify purchasing — Bradley handles the carrier claim (see Special Situations).

Supplies, DTG ink & the Amazon exception

Non-garment supplies (ink, thread, transfers, patches, leatherette, Uline boxes, JDS blanks, office/maintenance items) also need a PO. Receive them within 24 hours and route to the right department.

  • DTG ink from Kornit (ordered by Erik): verify against the PO, inspect for leaks, mark received, then write the PO # + date received on every bottle with a permanent marker. Store FIFO (new stock behind old — ink expires) and tell Brian.
  • No PO found (non-Amazon vendor): set it aside and notify the likely purchaser (Bradley, or Erik for Kornit ink) before doing anything else.
  • Amazon packages addressed to a person: do not open them — they're personal. Hand them to the named employee. But if an Amazon box is unusually large/heavy or looks like blank apparel stock, don't open it — check with Bradley or Ruthie first; it may be a business drop-ship.
Ordering from SanMar? Use the Attention line — Customer Name + logo type (DTG / Embroidery / Screenprint). It makes the package much faster to identify when it lands on the dock.

3 · Shipping

Shipping priority order

Pull a schedule of everything that has to ship today first thing in the morning, then work the queue in order. Address problems early — not at 3:42 in the afternoon.

1 · Rushes & events→ 2 · Past dues→ 3 · Replacements→ 4 · Back orders→ 5 · Web orders→ 6 · Normal scheduled
"Best way" is not a ship method. If an order reaches you with no method, check ShopWorks for an updated address, then contact the CSR who wrote it; if they're unavailable, contact the customer.

Step-by-step: shipping an order

  1. Verify the live ship-to in ShopWorks. The printed work order may be weeks old and the account executive may have changed the address or method since — always confirm against ShopWorks before you print a label.
  2. Pick the carrier — UPS, FedEx, or USPS, based on destination, size, weight, and speed. PO Boxes must ship USPS (UPS and FedEx can't deliver to a PO Box).
  3. Pack it and print the label. Add the customer's email so the carrier emails them the tracking number.
  4. Add the $4.50/box handling fee. UPS and FedEx add it to the freight automatically; on USPS / Stamps.com you must add it by hand. Add the freight + handling to the invoice in ShopWorks.
  5. Mark shipped in ShopWorks with the cost + tracking number, then put the box in the designated ship area for carrier pickup.
  6. Turn paperwork in to billing. Verify the shipping amount and handling fee, then hand the work order to billing.

USPS / Stamps.com tips

  • Labels print from the white Zebra thermal label printer.
  • Priority Mail for ~99% of corporate orders; First Class for items under 1 lb (Shopify / InkSoft tees).
  • Postage is live on Erik's credit card — weigh the box carefully, because a misprinted label means you have to request a refund.
  • The scale doesn't talk to Stamps.com — enter the weight by hand. (The DYMO scale does work with ShipStation.)
  • Remember to add the $4.50/box handling and enter the customer's email for tracking.

Rush, COD & prepaid-with-freight

  • Rush: ship UPS Next Day Air / 2nd Day Air — UPS comes daily, so no pickup call is needed. For FedEx priority (next-day / 2nd-day) you must call FedEx for pickup (they use a different truck).
  • Shipping on a customer's account: confirm the account isn't closed — if it is, we eat the freight.
  • COD: we can't ship COD without payment in hand.
  • Prepaid orders with freight: easiest is to tell the customer up front that freight is added after shipment. If we need to be paid in full, estimate the freight and add ~20% wiggle room. If actual freight comes in more than $20 over the estimate, notify the sales rep and bookkeeper to bill the difference.

4 · Packing

Pack it safely and professionally

  1. Gather supplies — right-sized boxes/bags, bubble wrap, tape, scissors, markers (clean and undamaged).
  2. Wrap delicate items individually in tissue or plastic, with the embroidered design visible so the client can inspect on arrival.
  3. Pack the box — line the bottom with bubble wrap/paper, stack the wrapped items neatly, and fill voids so nothing shifts.
  4. Label & document — client name, order number, and any packing list / invoice inside.
  5. Seal & shake-test — tape all openings, gently shake; if anything moves, add filler.
  6. Special requests — gift wrap or branded packaging if requested; "Fragile" labels for fragile goods; customs forms for international.
Shipping to Alaska? Boxes take a rough trip north — bag the items in plastic before they go in the box (keeps moisture/dirt out if the box gets beat up) and tape the box up well.

5 · Customer Pickups & Payments

When a customer comes to collect

  1. Stage the box on the Customer Pickup shelf — boxes are arranged alphabetically by customer name. Note on the order's front page: "Date Stamped: In Customer Pickup."
  2. Pull the order in ShopWorks and check the balance — do they still owe money?
  3. Collect before release. If there's a balance, take payment before the order leaves. Run the credit-card terminal (insert / tap / swipe) or take a check. (Already paid online? Just note it and release.)
  4. Record the payment in ShopWorks so the balance clears to zero.
  5. Close it out — mark the order picked up / shipped in ShopWorks, note on the front page that the customer picked up, and have the customer sign the pickup book.
Unsure about a balance or how to apply a payment? Ask Bradley (accounting) before releasing the order.
Switching a pickup to a shipment? Let the customer know the freight amount and add the shipping to the invoice.

6 · Closing the Day

End-of-day routine

  1. Import carrier tracking. In ShopWorks, run Import via ODBC → Today Only. This pulls the day's FedEx tracking onto the work orders. We don't have UPS integrated, so the "UPS didn't import" error is normal — click past it. Verify by opening a work order and checking the Shipping tab.
  2. Check the Customer Pickup clipboard / shelf for anything missed.
  3. Stage all outgoing packages so the carrier can pick them up.
  4. Tidy the area and clock out in TimeClick.

7 · Special Situations

Shipping address changes mid-process

When an address changes after an order is already in production, a printed work order with the wrong address is floating around and must be destroyed.

  • The sales rep updates the new address in ShopWorks and emails the production + shipping departments to destroy the old printed work order.
  • Shipping always re-verifies that the address in ShopWorks matches the printed work order before shipping — to be safe.

Returning samples to SanMar

  • Enter the order type "Sample Return to Vendor."
  • Charge the 6-piece embroidery price as a MEMO bill so it's clear the customer pays if the sample isn't returned.
  • The customer may keep the sample 1 week; we have 30 days to return it to SanMar. The customer ships it back to us and pays the freight.
  • Return the entire sample order — do not pull pieces to add to the order — and get an RA number from your SanMar rep first.

Forma Construction — bi-weekly shipping

A standing program for a single customer — not part of the daily flow.

  • Orders from the 1st–10th ship on the 15th; orders from the 11th–25th ship on the 30th (last day of month).
  • Bag & label each employee's order separately by name (e.g., "Jill Williams: 1 fleece pullover").
  • Combine all individual orders into one master box with a detailed packing list.
  • Ship via the cheapest reliable carrier (FedEx or USPS) and bill Forma the exact freight, no markup.
  • Email the tracking number + a copy of the packing list to Jennifer Harper.

Screen prints — coordinate with Ed

Ed at L&P Printing runs our screen prints. You prep, pack, log, and count — Erik drives them over and brings them back.

  • Prep & pack the screen-print orders; make sure every piece is packed.
  • Count all pieces and boxes before they leave so you have a starting number.
  • Log everything in the screen-print log (with Erik): what's at Ed's and how many boxes. Coordinate due dates with Ed and tell Erik when orders are ready for pickup.
  • When orders return, count every piece again and reconcile the log so nothing is missing.

UPS missing / damaged boxes & FOB terms

Custom apparel can't be returned or restocked, so every lost or damaged shipment is a complete loss. We ship FOB Shipping Point, Milton, WA — title and risk transfer to the buyer when the carrier accepts the shipment, so the customer technically owns the goods in transit. Even so, we usually help make it right.

  • Document before it ships: photograph completed items with the order sheet, close-ups of the print/embroidery, the items going into the box, and the sealed box with the label visible. This proves condition and is required for claims.
  • If a box is lost or damaged: photograph everything (item, packaging, the 1Z label, the outer box) and give it to Bradley, who files the carrier claim.
  • Deadlines: UPS allows 60 days to file loss/damage claims (but file within ~30 days for the best success rate); 15 days for late-delivery (service-guarantee) refunds. UPS's default liability is only $100/package.
Order valueWhat we do
Under $200Absorb the loss, replace immediately (admin time exceeds recovery)
$200 – $500Case-by-case — file if documentation is easy
Over $500Always file a claim; add declared value at shipment. Resolve the customer right away — don't let the claim delay them

8 · Vacation / Coverage Hand-Off

Before the clerk leaves

Main priority: the departing clerk sits with their cover (e.g., Ruthie), finishes pending tasks together, and walks the checklist below to confirm everything's covered.
Requesting the time off is a separate, company-wide process. Submit vacation requests in writing two weeks ahead and follow the approval / coverage workflow in the Vacation Scheduling Policy (2026) and Employee Handbook Ch. 13 — Leave & Time Off (the authority on PTO). The steps below are the dock-specific hand-off; for the company-wide coverage rules see Team Coverage During Absences.
  1. Set every login for the cover — Computer/PC, Stamps.com, ShipStation, UPS, ShopWorks (see Systems & Logins).
  2. Confirm payments are covered — how to run the credit-card terminal and record a payment in ShopWorks.
  3. Walk the floor together — Customer Pickup shelf, production staging, hold / return shelf, and supplies.
  4. Document open items — pending shipments, expected deliveries with dates, and anything that needs follow-up. Hand off each open task to a specific person.
  5. Set an out-of-office reply directing inquiries to the covering clerk.
